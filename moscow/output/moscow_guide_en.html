OTIUM

OTIUM is the practice of deliberate leisure. In the classical sense, otium is not escape from work but time when attention returns to memory, landscape, and thought — without a utilitarian deadline.

We shape routes for lingering, not for checklist tourism. The goal is presence in a place rather than consumption of sights.

OTIUM is not a tour operator. It is an invitation to walk, pause, and leave a route unfinished if the light on a façade deserves another minute.

Historical overview

↑ Contents

Moscow is the capital of Russia and one of the world's largest metropolises — a city with a thousand-year history that has evolved from a small settlement into a global economic, political and cultural hub.

History of foundation and development. The official founding date of Moscow is 1147 — this is when the city was first mentioned in the Ipatiev Chronicle. Prince Yuri Dolgoruky invited an ally to meet "in Moskov".

In the 13th–14th centuries Moscow becomes an important outpost during the Mongol invasion and avoids complete destruction. Under Prince Daniil Alexandrovich, the borders of the Moscow Principality start to expand. In 1325 the relocation of the Metropolitan of the Russian Orthodox Church from Vladimir to Moscow turns the city into the spiritual centre of the Russian lands. In 1367 the construction of the first white-stone Kremlin under Prince Dmitry Donskoy significantly strengthens the city's defences.

In 1480 the Great Stand on the Ugra River marks the final liberation from the Mongol-Tatar yoke and establishes Moscow as the capital of an independent state. In 1547 Ivan IV the Terrible's coronation in the Assumption Cathedral of the Kremlin makes Moscow the capital of the Tsardom of Russia. In 1612 the city is liberated by the militia of Minin and Pozharsky, marking the end of the Time of Troubles.

In 1712 the capital is moved to Saint Petersburg, but Moscow retains the status of the "first-throned" city and remains an important cultural centre. In 1812 occupation by Napoleon's army and a devastating fire are followed by rapid recovery; Moscow becomes a centre of Russian industry and crafts.

In 1918 Moscow regains its status as the capital — first of Soviet Russia, then of the USSR. In 1935 the first line of the Moscow Metro opens — one of the most beautiful metro systems in the world. In 1941–1942 the Battle of Moscow becomes one of the key battles of the Great Patriotic War that halted the advance of Nazi forces. In 1980 hosting the XXII Summer Olympic Games leads to large-scale infrastructure upgrades.

Since the 1990s Moscow has transformed into a global business and financial centre: Moscow City rises on the skyline, public spaces are renewed, and tourist and cultural infrastructure expands.

Tourist appeal. Moscow ranks among the top 20 most visited cities in Europe. The city attracts millions of tourists thanks to its rich history, cultural heritage and modern infrastructure.

The Kremlin, Red Square and other iconic sites form the core of any historical route. The Tretyakov Gallery, Pushkin Museum, Cosmonautics Museum and dozens of other institutions offer extensive collections. Ancient Russian churches, Baroque buildings, Stalinist skyscrapers and the towers of Moscow City coexist in the same panorama.

The Bolshoi Theatre, concert halls, festivals and exhibitions make the capital a magnet for art lovers. Zaryadye Park, Gorky Park, VDNH and Losiny Ostrov offer spaces for recreation and long walks. Parades, festivals such as "Moscow Seasons", and sporting events create a distinctive atmosphere in every season.

Must-visit attractions. The Moscow Kremlin is a symbol of Russian statehood, housing cathedrals, palaces and the Armoury Chamber — home to many of the country's most important historical and cultural monuments.

Red Square is the main square of the country, featuring St Basil's Cathedral, the GUM department store and Lenin's Mausoleum; major state and festive events take place here. St Basil's Cathedral is an architectural masterpiece of the 16th century with colourful domes, built to commemorate the capture of Kazan — one of the most recognisable symbols of Russia.

The Tretyakov Gallery holds the largest collection of Russian art (over 130,000 items) from the 11th to the 21st century: icons, paintings, graphics and sculpture. The Bolshoi Theatre is one of the world's leading opera and ballet venues, opened in 1825; the building is an example of classical architecture.

Zaryadye Park is a modern public space with panoramic views of the Kremlin and a "floating" bridge. Moscow City is a business district with skyscrapers, observation decks and restaurants overlooking the city. VDNH (the Exhibition of Achievements of the National Economy) is a vast ensemble of pavilions, fountains and museums — a symbol of the Soviet era and a full-day destination.

Current tourism trends. Moscow is developing gastronomic tourism (Russian cuisine, food courts, festivals), industrial tourism (historic factories and plants), ecological tourism (national parks, cycling routes, eco-trails), digital tourism (mobile guides, virtual tours, interactive exhibits) and thematic routes — literary, film and music tours, and walks through sites of historical events.

Tips for tourists. The best time to visit is May to September (warm weather) or December (festive New Year decorations); spring and summer bring many festivals, while winter offers a celebratory atmosphere.

Use the metro, buses and taxis for getting around; pedestrian zones on Arbat and Nikolskaya Street are ideal for walking. Book museum tickets (especially for the Kremlin and Tretyakov Gallery) and theatre performances in advance. Hotels in the centre — Kitai-Gorod or Tverskaya Street — keep major sights within reach; proximity to metro stations simplifies travel. Mobile maps, audio guides and tourist information centres help with orientation and route planning.

Moscow blends centuries-old history with dynamic modern life, offering travellers a unique experience — from immersion in the past to the latest trends of a global metropolis.

Buildings 11

↑ Contents

Address: Охотный ряд, 1

Until 1993 — Supreme Soviet.

Built between 1932 and 1935 according to the design of A. Ya. Langman for the Council of Labour and Defense (STO). The style is Soviet Neoclassicism: columns, rustication, and a symmetrical facade. The building is located in the city center, opposite the entrance to the Alexander Garden. From 1993, it was the site of the Supreme Soviet of the USSR and RSFSR; from 1994, it was the State Duma of the Russian Federation; the building of the Federation Council is nearby. The State Duma is the building of Russia's parliament; a symbol of legislative power; a monument of Soviet official architecture from the 1930s; and a key point on the political map of Moscow.

It was established by the Constitution of the Russian Federation in 1993.

Buildings 12

↑ Contents

Address: Манежная пл., 1

One of the largest unsupported halls of its time. Fire March 14, 2004 — two people died.

Built in 1817 by the design of O. I. Bobov for cavalry parades and reviews celebrating the victory over Napoleon. The wooden trusses system by A. Ya. Carbonie spanned a 45-meter span without intermediate supports—a record for that time. Since the 19th century, it has been used as an exhibition and concert hall. On March 14, 2004, a fire destroyed the roof and ceilings over several hours; two people died. Restoration took approximately one year; the interiors and exhibition function were recreated. Manège is the central exhibition hall of Moscow; a monument of classicism and engineering art; a symbol of recovery after the 2004 fire; and a venue for major exhibitions and public events.

It is the site of Moscow Design Museum since 2012.

Gatherings and concerts took place here.

The Dolgorukov princes' manor (18th century) was rebuilt multiple times. In 1784, the Noble (gentry) Club opened here, featuring the famous Column Hall. The hall with white columns is one of the largest in the country (with no central supports). It has historically been the site of congresses of the RCP(b)—VKP(b), concerts (Rachmaninoff, Shalyapin), and farewells to Stalin, Lenin, and Vysotsky; today, it hosts concerts, forums, and official ceremonies. The House of Unions (Colonnade Hall) is a venue for key official and cultural events in Russia; a historical and architectural monument; and a symbol of Moscow’s public and political life over more than two centuries.

Buildings 27

↑ Contents

Address: ул. Варварка, 4а

English Trade House.

The pavilions were erected in the late 15th–16th century. Since 1556, this location served as the residence of the English Moscow Company (handling trade and diplomatic missions). It is the oldest civil building in Moscow outside the Kremlin walls, and was restored in 1992–1994 based on the project by P. D. Baranovskiy. Since 1994, it has functioned as the "Old English Court" museum, featuring an exhibition on Russian-English relations. The Old English Court is the oldest civil building in Moscow outside the Kremlin walls; a monument to diplomatic and trade ties with England; and a museum of history and architecture of the 16th–17th centuries located on Varvarka Street.

Buildings 28

↑ Contents

Address: Кривоарбатский пер., 10

Two embedded cylinders; over 60 hexagonal windows. Museum since 2014.

Built between 1927 and 1929 on the design of K. S. Melnikovskiy as a private residential-workshop. Melnikovskiy won the plot in the "zhil-tovarishchestvo" (residential co-op) and constructed the building from two recessed cylinders with hexagonal windows (more than 60); the frame is wooden. Inside are a workshop, living quarters, and a solarium on the roof. For many years, the house was dilapidated; since 2014, it has been a branch of the Shchusev Museum of Architecture, and tours are held. The Melnikovskaya House is a masterpiece of the Soviet avant-garde; the only residential building of this shape in the world; an example of constructivism and the architect's personal work; and a cultural heritage site.

It was built in 1927–1929 in Krivoarbatsky Lane in Moscow by the outstanding Soviet architect Konstantin Melnikov for himself and his family.

Buildings 3

↑ Contents

Address: Пресненская наб.

Elevation of "Federation" 374 m.

The construction of the international business center on Presnenskaya Embankment began in the 1990s, with the first towers completed in the 2000s. Federation Tower (374 m) is one of the tallest in Europe, and the complex includes "Evolution," "Oko," "Imperiya," "City of Capitals," and others. The area houses offices, hotels, apartments, shopping galleries, and observation decks. Moscow City forms the silhouette of the western entrance to the city. Moscow City is the main business cluster of Russia and a symbol of modern Moscow. Skyscrapers with glass façades form a visible group from afar; the area includes offices, hotels, restaurants, and public spaces.

The Government of Moscow first conceived the project in 1992, as a mixed development of office, residential, retail and entertainment facilities.

Buildings 32

↑ Contents

Address: ул. Стромынка, 6

The cantilevered balconies of the auditorium.

Built in 1927–1929 on the design of K. S. Melnikov for the club of municipal workers (tram drivers, etc.). Three cantilevered volumes of the auditorium are featured on the facade—they resemble gears in shape; inside are a hall for 800 seats and circular rooms. One of the main masterpieces of Soviet avant-garde; after the 2000s restoration, it operates again as a cultural center. Rusakov Palace of Culture—Melnikov’s Constructivism masterpiece; a monument of 1920s club construction; a model of architecture as the art of form; the projecting volumes of the hall represent a unique solution in world architecture.

Buildings 6

↑ Contents

Address: Театральный пр., 1/4

Mosaics after Vrubelev's sketches.

The hotel was built between 1899 and 1905 on the initiative of Savva Mamontov; the project authors were V. F. Valkot, L. N. Kekushev, and V. V. Voeykov. The mosaic "Princess Greza" and the facade panel were executed based on sketches by M. A. Vrubel and A. Ya. Golovin. In 1918, the banquet hall hosted sessions of the V All-Russian Congress of Soviets, where the first Constitution of the RSFSR was adopted. After restoration during the 1990s–2010s, it became a premium hotel. The Metropol is an outstanding example of Modernism, featuring ceramic and majolica decor on its facade. This historic hotel is located near Teatralnaya Square; the mosaics by Vrubel and Golovin are considered one of the calling cards of Moscow architecture.

It is the largest extant Moscow hotel built before the Russian Revolution of 1917.
