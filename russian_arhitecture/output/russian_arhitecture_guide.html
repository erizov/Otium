Historical overview

↑ Contents

From the early Rus cross-in-square churches to twenty-first-century glass towers, Russian architecture moved from sacred prototype to imperial parade, from avant-garde experiment to panel housing and back toward international neo-eclecticism.

The first stone cathedrals — Saint Sophia in Kyiv, Novgorod and Pskov churches — established the domed Byzantine model. Rus masons of the eleventh and twelfth centuries mastered brick and stone, adapting Byzantine proportions to local materials. Fifteenth- and sixteenth-century Moscow blended Italian portal motifs with tent-roof experiments; ornamental uzorochye and Naryshkin Baroque marked the decorative exuberance of the pre-Petrine and early Romanov eras.

With the foundation of Saint Petersburg, architecture became the language of European presence: Petrine and Elizabethan Baroque, Neoclassicism and Empire shaped ceremonial ensembles. In the nineteenth century Konstantin Thon codified Russo-Byzantine forms, while eclecticism and pseudo-Russian romanticism filled cities with museums, stations and arcades.

At the turn of the twentieth century Art Nouveau and Neo-Russian styles produced expressive silhouettes; Neoclassicism preserved the gravitas of the bourgeois city. After the revolution Constructivism sought a new life in glass and concrete, yet by the 1930s the turn toward monumental neoclassicism and Stalinist Empire brought skyscrapers, metro palaces and VDNKh pavilions.

Post-war mass construction introduced serial panel districts and Soviet modernism — from Ostankino to Brutalism. Since the late 1980s Postmodernism quotes the past ironically, while contemporary landmarks — Moscow City, Zaryadye, Lakhta Center — place Russia in a global architectural conversation.

This guide is organized into thirty-two styles and movements. Each chapter presents two or three landmark buildings with architect names and concise context. The sequence can be read chronologically as a history of how form, space and state ideology were imagined in stone, brick and steel.

1. Early Rus architecture (10th–17th c.)

↑ Contents

Stone architecture of Kyivan Rus grew at the intersection of Byzantine tradition and local workshops. The cross-in-square church, mosaic and fresco define the earliest surviving monuments.

Walls are massive, narthexes compact, domes often rest on pendentives. Interior image and mosaic matter as much as structure — architecture serves liturgy and princely power.

Kyiv, Novgorod and Vladimir are the main centres; techniques spread from here to later Muscovite building.

Leading architects

  • Byzantine masters — Brought the cross-in-square type and mosaic techniques to Kyiv and Novgorod.

2. Novgorod school (12th–15th c.)

↑ Contents

The Novgorod school is known for low drums, asymmetrical façades and ornamental brickwork. Churches relate closely to the market square and trade routes.

Builders save stone but enrich surfaces with brick — dentils, cornices, recessed arches. Frescoes often survive remarkably well.

The style flourishes in the 12th–15th centuries and influences later Pskov and Moscow work.

Leading architects

  • Peter Kitiros — Builder of the Church of the Transfiguration on Ilyina Street, famed for its frescoes by Theophanes the Greek.
  • Novgorod workshops — Passed down brickwork and low-drum dome techniques from generation to generation.

3. Pskov school (14th–16th c.)

↑ Contents

Pskov churches are compact in volume yet rich in belfries and wall bell towers. Composition is often tied to fortification.

Octagonal drums and kokoshniks echo Novgorod, but façades are even more restrained.

The Pskov school is key to the north-western branch of medieval Rus architecture in the 14th–16th centuries.

Leading architects

  • Sosson the Lame — Legendary Pskov builder linked to the tradition of bell towers and compact churches.
  • Pskov workshops — Known for concise volumes, octagonal belfries and structures attached to fortress walls.

The Pskov Kremlin (Krom) is the ancient core of the city on a high cape at the confluence of rivers. The fortress grew from the tenth century; most stone walls and towers date from the fifteenth to seventeenth centuries. The ensemble includes Trinity Cathedral, Dovmont's Town and the Prikaz Chambers. A symbol of Pskov's defensive history and the focus of the city's past.

4. Muscovite architecture (15th–16th c.)

↑ Contents

Muscovite architecture of the 15th–16th centuries joins Russian dome composition with Italian portals and brick-and-limestone technique.

Kremlin cathedrals set a canon for the realm; masters such as Fioravanti and Aloisio bring classical proportion into Orthodox churches.

The same era sees early tent-roof experiments and richer façade ornament.

Leading architects

  • Aristotele Fioravanti — Italian architect at Ivan III's court; rebuilt the Kremlin Dormition Cathedral.
  • Aloisio the New — Designed the Archangel Cathedral and introduced Italian motifs into the Moscow Kremlin.
  • Postnik Yakovlev — Traditionally credited with Kremlin cathedrals and major sixteenth-century church ensembles.

Archangel Cathedral of the Moscow Kremlin

↑ Contents

Address: Москва | Style: Muscovite architecture (15th–16th c.) | Period: 1508

The Archangel Cathedral of the Moscow Kremlin is the necropolis of grand princes and tsars, built by Aloisio the New. Built in 1505–1508 by the Italian architect Aloisio the New on the site of an earlier cathedral. Until the capital moved to St Petersburg it was the main burial church of Muscovite rulers from Ivan Kalita to the early Romanovs; more than fifty tombs and a 17th-century iconostasis survive inside. 5. Tent-roof style (16th c.)

↑ Contents

The tent-roof style is a bold mid-16th-century Moscow experiment: churches without interior pillars, with a tall tent instead of a dome.

Saint Basil's Cathedral is the era's symbol; each tower is a separate chapel on a shared basement.

Tents stress verticality and festivity, linked to Ivan the Terrible's victories and the idea of a heavenly city.

Leading architects

  • Barma and Postnik — Folk tradition links them to Saint Basil's Cathedral on Red Square.
  • Moscow tent-roof masters — Experimented with octagons and tent roofs without internal pillars — rare in Rus.

Saint Basil's Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Москва | Style: Tent-roof style (16th c.) | Period: 1561

The Intercession Cathedral on the Moat, known as Saint Basil's, is the chief masterpiece of 16th-century Muscovite tent-roof design. Founded in 1555–1561 by Ivan IV to commemorate the capture of Kazan. Nine churches on a common platform create the festive skyline of Red Square; the central tower echoes Kolomenskoye's Ascension tent. A unique ensemble of tents, domes and vivid façade painting.

Church of the Ascension in Kolomenskoye

The Ascension Church in Kolomenskoye is the earliest stone tent-roof church in Russia and a landmark of 1530s Moscow. Built in 1532 to mark the birth of Tsarevich Ivan (the future Ivan the Terrible). The tent form became the emblem of Muscovite architectural experiment under Ivan IV.

6. Muscovite ornamental style (17th c.)

↑ Contents

Muscovite ornamental architecture of the 17th century layers kokoshniks, cornices and window surrounds in the Time of Troubles and early Romanov era.

Churches and terem chambers are jewelled with ornament; planning stays traditional while the façade becomes a textile of pattern.

Uzorochye anticipates Naryshkin Baroque and keeps ties to princely Moscow.

Leading architects

  • Bazhen Ogurtsov — A leading ornamental-style master; worked on terem chambers and church ornament.
  • Osip Startsev — Built churches rich in kokoshnik ornament in Moscow and its region.

7. Naryshkin Baroque (late 17th c.)

↑ Contents

Naryshkin Baroque is Moscow 'baroque under the dome': vertical kokoshnik tiers and white-stone carving of the late 17th century.

Churches appear light and soaring though built of brick and limestone.

The style bridges ornamental Muscovy and Petrine modernisation.

Leading architects

  • Yakov Bukhvostov — Leading master of 'baroque under the dome': vertical kokoshnik tiers and white-stone carving.
  • Peter Potapov — Designed the Intercession Church at Fili — a hallmark of Naryshkin Baroque.
  • Osip Startsev — Combined refined façades with traditional Russian church planning.

8. Petrine Baroque (early 18th c.)

↑ Contents

Petrine Baroque shapes the new capital on the Neva: canal grid, fortress, cathedral and Northern Baroque palaces.

Trezzini and Le Blond lay out the 'regular city' — architecture serves an imperial project of reform.

Petersburg becomes a showcase of Europe with its own scale and climate strategies.

Leading architects

  • Domenico Trezzini — Laid out Petersburg's grid; designed Peter and Paul Cathedral and early stone ensembles.
  • Jean-Baptiste Le Blond — Planned ceremonial vistas and palace ensembles along the Neva.
  • Giacomo Quarenghi — Introduced strict Neoclassicism in the Smolny Institute and palace buildings.

Lakhta Center is a supertall tower and Gazprom headquarters on the Gulf of Finland shore. At 462 metres, it was completed in 2018 to a design by RMJM (Tony Kettle). A twisting façade and public spaces at the base add a new focal point to the city skyline. Europe's tallest building and a symbol of contemporary St Petersburg.
