From the early Rus cross-in-square churches to twenty-first-century glass towers, Russian architecture moved from sacred prototype to imperial parade, from avant-garde experiment to panel housing and back toward international neo-eclecticism.

The first stone cathedrals — Saint Sophia in Kyiv, Novgorod and Pskov churches — established the domed Byzantine model. Rus masons of the eleventh and twelfth centuries mastered brick and stone, adapting Byzantine proportions to local materials. Fifteenth- and sixteenth-century Moscow blended Italian portal motifs with tent-roof experiments; ornamental uzorochye and Naryshkin Baroque marked the decorative exuberance of the pre-Petrine and early Romanov eras.

With the foundation of Saint Petersburg, architecture became the language of European presence: Petrine and Elizabethan Baroque, Neoclassicism and Empire shaped ceremonial ensembles. In the nineteenth century Konstantin Thon codified Russo-Byzantine forms, while eclecticism and pseudo-Russian romanticism filled cities with museums, stations and arcades.

At the turn of the twentieth century Art Nouveau and Neo-Russian styles produced expressive silhouettes; Neoclassicism preserved the gravitas of the bourgeois city. After the revolution Constructivism sought a new life in glass and concrete, yet by the 1930s the turn toward monumental neoclassicism and Stalinist Empire brought skyscrapers, metro palaces and VDNKh pavilions.

Post-war mass construction introduced serial panel districts and Soviet modernism — from Ostankino to Brutalism. Since the late 1980s Postmodernism quotes the past ironically, while contemporary landmarks — Moscow City, Zaryadye, Lakhta Center — place Russia in a global architectural conversation.

This guide is organized into thirty-two styles and movements. Each chapter presents two or three landmark buildings with architect names and concise context. The sequence can be read chronologically as a history of how form, space and state ideology were imagined in stone, brick and steel.
