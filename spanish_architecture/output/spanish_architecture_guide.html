Historical overview

↑ Contents

Iberian architecture blends Roman heritage, Islamic Al-Andalus, Christian kingdoms and Portugal's maritime empire—from Segovia to Lisbon.

Roman Hispania and Visigothic churches gave way to Córdoba's mosques and the Alhambra; Mudéjar joined Islamic ornament with Christian volumes. Catalan and Isabelline Gothic, Manueline and Plateresque reflected the Age of Discovery; Herrera's Escorial and Churrigueresque set Spain's Baroque fervour.

Gaudí's Catalan Modernisme and Portuguese Art Nouveau, interwar Rationalism and dictatorship monumentalism prepared post-war modernization and contemporary museums such as the Guggenheim Bilbao.

This guide covers Spain and Portugal in chronological chapters with building examples and brief notes.

1. Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

↑ Contents

Aqueducts, theatres and bridges of Roman Iberia.

Aqueduct of Segovia

↑ Contents

Address: Сеговия | Style: Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

Two-tier aqueduct, mortar-free upper section. About 28.5 m high; 167 granite arches, dry-jointed in the upper tier. Peak of Roman Iberia. UNESCO since 1985; carried water from the Frío river.

Italica amphitheatre

↑ Contents

Address: Сантипонсе | Style: Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

Amphitheatre and veteran Roman town. Amphitheatre for about 25,000; town for veterans of the Second Spanish Legion. Roman colonisation of Iberia. Birthplace of emperors Trajan and Hadrian; excavated near Santiponce.

Bridge of Alcántara

↑ Contents

Address: Алькантара | Style: Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

Tagus bridge with triumphal arch. Six arches over the Tagus; triumphal arch dedicated to Emperor Trajan. Roman engineering. Built c. 104 CE by the engineer Gaius Julius Lacer.

Tarragona amphitheatre

↑ Contents

Address: Таррагона | Style: Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

Amphitheatre on the Mediterranean shore. Second-century amphitheatre on the sea slope; arena for gladiatorial games. Roman Tarraco. Part of Roman Tarraco, capital of Hispania Citerior; UNESCO site.

Theatre of Mérida

↑ Contents

Address: Мерида | Style: Roman Hispania (1st–5th c.)

Roman theatre with stage wall. Marble scaenae frons stage; seated about six thousand spectators. UNESCO Roman Spain. Founded under Augustus; core of Augusta Emerita, capital of Lusitania.

3. Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

↑ Contents

Mosques, palaces and gardens of Córdoba and Granada.

Alhambra

↑ Contents

Address: Гранада | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Nasrid palace with Hall of Two Sisters. Nasrid palaces on the Assabica hill; stucco, muqarnas and inscription bands. Peak of Islamic Iberia. UNESCO site; symbol of Granada and the 1492 Reconquista.

↑ Contents

Address: Севилья | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Mudéjar palace of Peter the Cruel. Patio de las Doncellas and Ambassadors' Hall with Mudéjar stucco. Islam and Christianity in stone. Still a royal residence; layers from Almohad to Pedro I of Castile.

↑ Contents

Address: Сарагоса | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Islamic palace in Zaragoza. Taifa palace of the Banu Hud; brick halls, arches and timber ceilings. Taifas and Mudéjar. Later an Aragonese royal seat; core of the Zaragoza Cortes.

Baños de la Magdalena

↑ Contents

Address: Кордова | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Islamic baths with domes. Islamic hammam with star-shaped light openings in stone vaults. Islamic civic architecture. Rare example of civic architecture in caliphal Córdoba.

Hall of Comares

↑ Contents

Address: Гранада | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Alhambra throne room. Throne room with muqarnas dome and the legendary throne by the window. Nasrid luxury. Political heart of the fourteenth-century Nasrid court.

Medina Azahara

↑ Contents

Address: Кордова | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.) | Period: 936–940

Caliphal palace of Abd al-Rahman III. Caliphal city west of Córdoba; Rich Hall and basilical throne room. Córdoba caliphate luxury. UNESCO site; ninth-century splendour of Abd al-Rahman III.

Great Mosque of Córdoba

↑ Contents

Address: Кордова | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.) | Period: 785–987

Mosque with forest of columns and red arches. Spolia columns; alternating red and white voussoirs rhythm the hall. Umayyad masterpiece in Europe. Expanded by al-Hakam II; later a Christian choir was inserted at centre.

Palacio del Partal

↑ Contents

Address: Гранада | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.)

Pavilion reflected in water. Arcaded portico reflected in the pool; a preserved garden pavilion. Nasrid garden design. One of the earliest surviving Nasrid structures in the Alhambra.

Torre del Oro

↑ Contents

Address: Севилья | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.) | Period: 1221

Twelve-sided watchtower. Dodecagonal tower on the Guadalquivir; linked to a chain across the river. Seville Islamic architecture. Built under Almohad rule; now a maritime museum.

Giralda

↑ Contents

Address: Севилья | Style: Islamic architecture of Al-Andalus (8th–15th c.) | Period: 1184–1198

Former mosque minaret, cathedral tower. Almohad minaret about 104 m high; a Renaissance belfry was added later. Symbol of Al-Andalus. Former Great Mosque minaret; icon of the Seville skyline.
