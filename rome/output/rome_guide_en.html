OTIUM

OTIUM is the practice of deliberate leisure. In the classical sense, otium is not escape from work but time when attention returns to memory, landscape, and thought — without a utilitarian deadline.

We shape routes for lingering, not for checklist tourism. The goal is presence in a place rather than consumption of sights.

OTIUM is not a tour operator. It is an invitation to walk, pause, and leave a route unfinished if the light on a façade deserves another minute.

Appian Way

↑ Contents

Period: 4th century BC

The Appian Way is one of the oldest and most famous ancient roads in Rome, dating back to the 4th century BC. Originally constructed as a military road connecting Rome with Brindisi on the Adriatic coast, it later became a major trade route and a symbol of Roman imperial power. It spans approximately 150 miles (240 km), stretching from Rome to Brindisi. Its name derives from 'via Appia,' meaning 'Road of Appius.'. Many notable figures, including Julius Caesar and Hannibal, traveled along this route. Remains of ancient structures such as tombs and villas can still be seen today. In modern times, parts of the Appian Way have been converted into scenic walking paths.

Built by Appius Claudius Caecus around 312 BC, the Appian Way was initially designed for military purposes but soon evolved into a vital artery for commerce and communication across Italy. Over time, it grew into a grandiose monumental road lined with tombs, villas, and monuments, reflecting the wealth and influence of Rome's ruling class. Today, the Appian Way serves as a historical reminder of Rome’s past glory and its enduring legacy as a hub of culture and commerce.

Ara Pacis Museum

↑ Contents

Address: Lungotevere in Augusta | Style: Contemporary museum encasing ancient altar | Period: 2006

The Ara Pacis Museum is a modern architectural marvel housing one of Rome's most significant archaeological treasures—the Altar of Peace. Located on the banks of the Tiber River, it showcases ancient Roman artifacts and provides insight into the city's rich historical heritage. Richard Meier glass-and-stone pavilion sheltering Augustus' altar of peace reliefs. Housed within the museum are fragments of the original Altar of Peace dating back to the time of Emperor Augustus. The museum features innovative design elements such as natural light filtering through skylights, enhancing the display of artifacts. It stands as a testament to the fusion of classical and modern architectural styles. Exhibits include sculptures, reliefs, and frescoes depicting scenes from Roman life and mythology. Located near the Palatine Hill and the Tiber River, the site offers scenic views of Rome’s historic center. Ticket separate from Forum passes. Exterior angles photograph cleanly in morning side-light.

Built in 13 BC to celebrate peace under Augustus' reign, the original altar was discovered during excavations in the early 20th century. The museum opened in 2006, combining ancient ruins with contemporary architecture designed by Richard Meier, blending classical elements with modern aesthetics. Altar founded 9 BCE; 20th-century excavation and controversial modern wrapping. Visiting the Ara Pacis Museum allows visitors to experience Rome’s history firsthand through its preserved artifacts and stunning architecture. Augustan propaganda in botanical friezes—telluric calm after civil wars.

Arch of Constantine

↑ Contents

Address: Beside Colosseum | Style: Late-antique triumphal arch | Period: 315 AD

The Arch of Constantine is a monumental triumphal arch located at the start of the Via Triumphalis, celebrating Emperor Constantine's victory over Maxentius in the Battle of Milvian Bridge in 312 AD. Imperial triumphal arch recycling earlier reliefs—political collage beside the amphitheatre. Constructed during the reign of Emperor Constantine I. Consists of three arches, with the central one being larger than the side ones. Features intricate marble friezes and reliefs depicting significant moments from Roman history. Located near the Colosseum and Palatine Hill, making it easily accessible within the heart of Rome’s historic center. Symbolizes the beginning of the Via Triumphalis, a route used for triumphant processions. Cross traffic on foot carefully toward the forum. Read panels to spot Hadrianic hunting roundels reused.

Built around 315 AD to commemorate Emperor Constantine’s decisive victory, the Arch of Constantine was constructed by combining elements from earlier Roman monuments and temples. It stands as a testament to the fusion of classical architecture with Christian symbolism, featuring reliefs depicting scenes from both pagan and Christian narratives. Dedicated 315 CE after Milvian Bridge victory; spolia study set for art historians. A must-visit for its historical significance and architectural ingenuity, the Arch of Constantine offers a unique glimpse into ancient Rome’s transition from paganism to Christianity. Framing element in almost every classic Colosseum postcard.

Archbasilica of St. John Lateran

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza di San Giovanni in Laterano | Style: Early Christian / Baroque | Period: 4th century; Baroque façade 1732–1735

The cathedral church of Rome and official ecclesiastical seat of the Pope, crowned by an ornate Baroque façade overlooking Piazza di San Giovanni in Laterano. Houses the Scala Santa, steps brought from Jerusalem according to tradition. The Lateran obelisk in the piazza is the tallest standing ancient obelisk in Rome. Interior bronze doors were taken from the Roman Curia in the Forum. Baptistery beside the basilica is among the oldest in Christendom. Dress modestly; active worship continues alongside tourism.

Emperor Constantine donated the site; the present basilica descends from a 4th-century foundation rebuilt after fires and restorations across the Middle Ages and Renaissance. Borromini reshaped the interior; Alessandro Galilei designed the monumental white façade in the 1730s. Mother church of all churches in Rome and the world; pilgrims begin here before other major basilicas.

Basilica of Saint Paul Outside the Walls

↑ Contents

Style: Early Christian and Late Antique | Period: 4th century AD

The Basilica of Saint Paul Outside the Walls is a majestic Roman basilica, standing outside the ancient city walls, dedicated to St. Paul the Apostle. It’s one of Rome's most significant Christian sites and a testament to medieval architecture. It is located just outside the Aurelian Walls, reflecting its original purpose as a burial site. The basilica has undergone several renovations over the centuries, with notable restorations completed in the 17th and 19th centuries. Its interior features impressive mosaics and frescoes, including works by Renaissance artists such as Raphael and Perin del Vaga. Saint Paul's remains are believed to be buried beneath the high altar, making it a sacred destination for pilgrims. The basilica also houses relics of Saints Lucia and Stephen.

Built in the 4th century AD by Emperor Constantine, the basilica was originally constructed as a mausoleum for St. Paul. Over time, it evolved into a church, becoming one of the four major papal basilicas in Rome. Its design reflects both early Christian and late antique architectural styles, showcasing the transition between pagan and Christian influences. This basilica is important for its historical significance and architectural value, offering visitors a glimpse into the early days of Christianity in Rome.

Basilica of San Clemente

↑ Contents

Style: Medieval, with elements of Roman architecture | Period: (4th century)

The Basilica of San Clemente is a remarkable underground complex beneath Rome's historic center. It combines layers of ancient ruins, medieval structures, and Renaissance art, offering visitors a unique glimpse into the city's rich history. It features three distinct levels: a modern church above ground, a medieval church below, and extensive ancient Roman ruins underneath. The lower level contains the oldest part of the structure, dating back to the 4th century. Notable highlights include the stunning mosaics and frescoes from various eras, which reflect different artistic styles. Archaeological excavations revealed evidence of a Roman domus (house), baths, and catacombs. The basilica has been designated a minor basilica by the Catholic Church.

Built over several centuries, the basilica was originally constructed as a Roman house around the 4th century AD. Over time, it became a Christian church dedicated to Saint Clement, one of the earliest popes. The site underwent significant renovations during the Middle Ages and later served as a parish church until the early 20th century. Today, its underground levels showcase intricate mosaics, frescoes, and archaeological remains that provide insights into Rome’s evolution through the ages. Visiting the Basilica of San Clemente allows travelers to explore a fascinating blend of historical periods, including Roman, Byzantine, and medieval influences.

Basilica of Santa Maria Maggiore

↑ Contents

Style: Early Christian and Renaissance | Period: 432–439 AD

Dominating the Piazza di Santa Maria Maggiore, this basilica is one of Rome's most iconic landmarks and a major center for Christian pilgrimage. It is considered one of the four major papal basilicas in Rome. The basilica features mosaics dating back to the 5th century that depict scenes from the life of Jesus and the Virgin Mary. Its dome, designed by Bramante, is notable for its octagonal shape and distinctive appearance. Santa Maria Maggiore is also known for its impressive façade decorated with sculptures by Bernini. Legend holds that the basilica was built on the site where snow fell during summer, indicating divine approval.

Built between 432 and 439 AD by Pope Sixtus III, the Basilica of Santa Maria Maggiore was originally constructed as a celebration of the Virgin Mary's apparition to St. Elena. It has been expanded and restored numerous times over the centuries, including significant renovations under Pope Paul V in the 17th century. The basilica remains a key site in Roman Catholic liturgy and art, housing important religious relics and works of art. A must-visit for its historical significance, architectural beauty, and spiritual importance.

Baths of Caracalla

↑ Contents

Address: Via delle Terme di Caracalla | Style: Severan Roman baths | Period: 212–216 AD

The Baths of Caracalla are among the largest and best-preserved ancient Roman bath complexes, offering a glimpse into the opulence and social life of imperial Rome. Imperial bath complex—vaulted halls, mosaic scraps, and summer opera staging. Constructed using travertine stone and marble, the baths spanned over 39 acres. They were equipped with heated water supplied by aqueducts, ensuring comfort for users. The baths featured intricate mosaics and sculptures, many of which have been restored and can be seen today. At their peak, they served up to 1,600 visitors daily. Today, visitors can explore the ruins and imagine what it was like to enjoy these luxurious facilities. Bring sun cover; little shade among high walls. Night visits when opera runs feel otherworldly.

Built during the reign of Emperor Caracalla around 212–216 AD, these baths were designed to serve as a hub for relaxation, socializing, and public health. They could accommodate thousands of people at once, showcasing advanced engineering and architectural techniques of the time. The complex included swimming pools, saunas, exercise rooms, and lavish decorations, reflecting the grandiose lifestyle of the Roman elite. Opened early 3rd century CE; water and spectacle on a neighbourhood scale. Visiting the Baths of Caracalla allows travelers to experience the grandeur of ancient Roman architecture and understand how Romans lived, worked, and entertained themselves. Open-air ruins teach Roman infrastructure without museum glass.

Baths of Diocletian

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza della Repubblica / Via Cernaia | Style: Late Roman baths / Renaissance church | Period: 298–306 CE; Michelangelo church 1560s

Imperial bath complex whose frigidarium and caldarium halls now host Santa Maria degli Angeli and the National Roman Museum collections. Meridian line and astronomical instruments inside the church transept. Cloister of Michelangelo frames a quiet courtyard. Combined tickets link other National Roman Museum sites. Repubblica metro station faces the exedra piazza. Near Termini station and the 19th-century fountain nymphaeum.

Diocletian's largest baths served thousands daily; Michelangelo converted sections into a church; cloisters and galleries display epigraphy and sculpture. Rare chance to stand inside bath halls that defined Roman leisure architecture.

Campo de' Fiori

↑ Contents

Style: Baroque

Campo de' Fiori is a vibrant square in Rome known for its lively morning market and historic significance. Located near the University of Rome, it has been a hub for intellectual exchange and political gatherings since medieval times. The morning market features fresh produce, flowers, and local crafts. It is one of the oldest markets in Rome, dating back to the Renaissance era. Nearby stands a statue commemorating Giordano Bruno's martyrdom. The square is surrounded by elegant Baroque buildings and narrow streets. On weekends, the market expands with additional stalls selling souvenirs and handicrafts.

Originally named after a Roman nobleman, Campo de' Fiori was once a marshy area used as a dumping ground. Over time, it became a popular meeting place for scholars and artists. In the 16th century, it gained prominence as a marketplace, hosting a daily flower and vegetable market that continues to attract visitors today. The square also holds historical importance due to the execution of Giordano Bruno, a philosopher and astronomer, who was burned at the stake here in 1600. Visitors come to Campo de' Fiori to experience the bustling market, enjoy the lively atmosphere, and reflect on its rich history.

Capitoline Hill

↑ Contents

Address: Campidoglio | Style: Renaissance civic replanning

Perched high above Rome's historic center, Capitoline Hill is one of the city's seven hills and a key symbol of ancient Roman power. It was originally home to temples dedicated to Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva, reflecting its role as the spiritual heart of the Roman Empire. Michelangelo's trapezoidal piazza, equestrian Marcus Aurelius, and palazzi that host the city museum. The hill is part of Rome's seven legendary hills, each with its own historical significance. It served as the center of Roman civic life and religion for centuries. Today, the Capitoline Museums showcase artifacts dating back to the Republic and Empire periods. Notable sculptures include the 'She-Wolf of Rome,' which symbolizes the founding of Rome by Romulus and Remus. Its location offers panoramic views over the city, providing a unique perspective on Rome's layout. Museum ticket includes palazzo courtyards. Bus groups peak late morning.

Founded during the early days of Rome, Capitoline Hill became the site of important religious and political structures. The Capitoline Museums, now located here, house some of the most significant artifacts from ancient Rome, including the famous 'Bull of Carystos' sculpture. Its prominence in Roman mythology and history makes it a must-visit for anyone interested in understanding the roots of Western civilization. Religious centre of archaic Rome; Renaissance replanning cleared medieval clutter. Visit Capitoline Hill to explore the birthplace of Roman religion and politics, and witness firsthand the art and architecture that shaped Western culture. Balcony over the Forum remains one of the best urban belvederes.

Capuchin Crypt

↑ Contents

Style: Baroque | Period: 1629–1677

The Capuchin Crypt is a unique and macabre underground chapel located beneath the Church of Santa Maria della Concezione dei Cappuccini in Rome. It houses the skeletal remains of over 4,000 friars, meticulously arranged into intricate patterns and scenes. It contains approximately 4,000 skeletons arranged in elaborate designs. The crypt's walls are decorated with bone chandeliers, benches, and even a large skull-covered altar. Each arrangement represents different themes such as resurrection, judgment day, and the transience of earthly existence. The chapel is part of a larger complex that includes the church above it, which serves as a traditional Catholic worship space. The crypt was originally intended for religious instruction and meditation on mortality.

Built between 1629 and 1677 by the Order of Friars Minor Capuchin, the crypt was designed to serve as a reminder of mortality and the transient nature of life. The bones were exhumed from various Capuchin cemeteries and creatively arranged to form artistic displays, including allegorical scenes depicting the stages of life and death. Visiting the Capuchin Crypt offers a rare opportunity to witness one of the most unusual and historically significant examples of mortuary art in Europe.

Castel Sant'Angelo

↑ Contents

Address: Lungotevere Castello | Style: Ancient mausoleum with medieval bastions | Period: 139 AD

Situated on the banks of the Tiber River, Castel Sant'Angelo was originally built as a mausoleum for Emperor Hadrian in the 2nd century AD. Over time it transformed into a fortress and later became a papal residence. Cylinder tomb of Hadrian turned fortress, prison, and museum—linked to the Vatican by the elevated passage. It is one of the best-preserved examples of Roman architecture dating back to the 2nd century. The castle features five towering cylindrical sections that symbolize the Five Wounds of Christ. During World War II, Mussolini's Fascist regime used it as a headquarters before being captured by Allied forces. Today, visitors can ascend to the top for panoramic vistas of Rome’s historic center. Its design influenced many subsequent fortifications across Europe. Reduced crowds after sunset openings on some dates. The angels' bridge is a separate photo subject.

Constructed by Emperor Hadrian around 139 AD to serve as his burial site, the structure evolved into a military stronghold during the Middle Ages. It was subsequently used as a prison and a castle by popes, including Pope Nikolay V who added defensive walls and towers. In 1527, it played a crucial role in defending Rome against attacks by Charles V's troops. Antiquity through papal refuge in sieges; angel statue gives city its name anecdote. A must-visit for its historical significance and stunning views overlooking the Tiber River and Vatican City. Terrace views along the Tiber bend toward the dome.

Catacombs of San Callisto

↑ Contents

Address: Via Appia Antica | Style: Early Christian catacombs | Period: 2nd–4th century CE

Largest and best-known Roman catacombs, a labyrinth of tunnels with papal and martyr tombs along the ancient Appian Way. Network exceeds 20 kilometres of passages. Sacramental chapels show early Christian symbols and frescoes. Combine with Appian Way park walk or San Sebastiano nearby. Closed Wednesdays and some feast days—check schedule. Modest dress required; photography rules vary.

Christians carved multilevel galleries for burials when in-ground cemetery space ran short; popes and early martyrs were interred in privileged crypts. Guided visits only—cool underground temperatures year-round.

Circus Maximus

↑ Contents

Address: Between Palatine and Aventine hills | Style: Ancient Roman circus | Period: 6th century BC origins; imperial rebuilding

Vast chariot-racing arena whose grassy basin still conveys the scale of spectacles that once drew hundreds of thousands of Romans. Could hold an estimated 150,000–250,000 spectators. Underground structures and shops line parts of the edge. Concerts and public events occasionally use the site today. Metro stop Circo Massimo opens onto the eastern curve. Pair with Aventine Keyhole or Rose Garden on the opposite hill.

Kings and emperors lengthened the track and raised the spina; political factions backed rival charioteer teams in races that could sway public mood. Free to walk the perimeter; superb views toward the Palatine and Aventine.

Colosseum

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza del Colosseo, Rome | Style: Ancient Roman concrete and travertine | Period: 70-80 AD

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is one of Rome's most iconic landmarks and a testament to ancient Roman engineering prowess. Huge Flavian amphitheatre—symbol of imperial Rome and still a reference for crowds, games, and engineering. Constructed between 70-80 AD. Ancient Romans referred to it as 'Amphitheatrum Flavium'. It hosted events such as animal hunts, mock sea battles, and executions. Today, it remains a symbol of Roman architecture and heritage. Restoration efforts continue to preserve its historical integrity. Combined tickets often include the Forum and Palatine. Underground hypogeum visits require guided slots.

Built during the first century AD under Emperor Vespasian, the Colosseum was designed for gladiatorial contests, public spectacles, and ceremonies. It could accommodate up to 50,000 spectators and featured complex hydraulic systems for raising animals and scenery. Its name derives from Nero’s nearby 'Golden House' ('Domus Aurea'), colloquially called 'Colossus'. Opened around 80 CE; quarry for builders in later ages; modern conservation balances access with structure stress. Visit the Colosseum to witness the grandeur of ancient Rome and understand its cultural significance. UNESCO heart of the historic centre; evenings emphasize silhouette and lit arches.

Galleria Borghese

↑ Contents

Address: Piazzale Scipione Borghese, Villa Borghese | Style: Roman Baroque villa museum | Period: Villa 1613–1616; museum since 1902

Cardinal Scipione Borghese's villa museum packed with Bernini marbles, Caravaggio paintings, and antiquities in richly decorated salons. Masterpieces include Caravaggio's Young Sick Bacchus and David with Goliath's Head. Canova's Pauline Bonaparte as Venus Victrix reclines in a dedicated room. Garden strolls extend visits through Villa Borghese park. No photos in some rooms—check current policy at entry. Reachable by bus or a walk through the park from Spanish Steps.

Bernini's Apollo and Daphne, Pluto and Proserpina, and David were conceived for these rooms; Napoleonic pressures scattered some antiquities to the Louvre. Timed two-hour entries limit crowds—book weeks ahead in peak season.

Largo di Torre Argentina

↑ Contents

Address: Campus Martius | Style: Republican Roman sacred architecture

Hidden beneath the bustling streets of Rome lies a tranquil oasis—a square where ancient ruins coexist with modern life. Largo di Torre Argentina is home to the remains of four Roman temples dedicated to the gods Jupiter, Juno, and Mars, dating back to the 3rd century BC. Sacred area with Republican temple pediments below street level—cat sanctuary in the excavation pit. The area was originally named after the nearby medieval tower, Torre Argentina. Excavated between 1929 and 1960, it has yielded significant artifacts and sculptures. Today, it's also famous for its cat sanctuary, where numerous feline residents roam freely among the ruins. Visitors can explore the site through guided tours or self-guided walks. Adjacent to the ruins stands the Church of Santa Maria del Rosario. Viewing platforms change with renovation phases. Jewish Ghetto restaurants border the block.

Discovered during excavations in the early 20th century, these ruins were once part of a vast complex known as the Regia, the official residence of Roman kings. The site gained international fame when archaeologists uncovered the remains of Julius Caesar's assassination site, now marked by a bronze statue. Temples A–D identified by debate; Caesar fell nearby in the Curia of Pompey's theatre. This historical site offers visitors a rare glimpse into ancient Roman civilization, blending archaeological significance with scenic charm. Archaeology meets feline charity under traffic roar.

Mausoleum of Augustus

↑ Contents

Style: Classical Roman | Period: 28 BC

The Mausoleum of Augustus is a majestic ancient monument, originally designed as the final resting place for Rome's first emperor and his family. It stands as one of the most iconic symbols of Roman imperial power and architectural grandeur. Constructed using limestone and marble, the structure originally had a circular design with a domed roof. It stood approximately 87 meters high when complete, making it one of the tallest structures in Rome at that time. Today, only the base and part of the outer wall remain, but these ruins still evoke the grandeur of ancient Rome. The site also includes archaeological excavations revealing more about the original layout and architecture. Its location on the Via Flaminia highlights its strategic importance within the city.

Built during the reign of Emperor Augustus around 28 BC, the mausoleum was intended to house his remains and those of future members of his dynasty. Designed by architect Agrippa, it served both as a tomb and a shrine to celebrate the new era of Roman rule under Augustus. Its massive scale and intricate decorations made it a focal point of Rome’s urban landscape. Visiting the Mausoleum of Augustus offers insight into the grandiose ambitions of the early Roman Empire and its enduring legacy.

Monte Testaccio

↑ Contents

Monte Testaccio is a massive archaeological site and man-made hill located on the outskirts of Rome. It rises above the Tiber River, offering panoramic views of the city below. It is one of the largest artificial mounds in Europe. The hill contains over 50 million pieces of pottery. Monte Testaccio has been designated as a national monument since 1928. Today, it serves as both a tourist attraction and a nature reserve. Its location makes it an ideal spot for birdwatching.

Built during the Roman Empire as a dumping ground for broken pottery and ceramics, Monte Testaccio was once a vast repository of amphora fragments. Over time, it grew into a significant landfill, reaching its current height of around 34 meters. The hill's name derives from 'testa' meaning head, referencing the ceramic heads that were commonly found here. Visiting Monte Testaccio provides insight into ancient Roman waste management practices and offers a unique perspective on the city's historical development.

Mouth of Truth

↑ Contents

Address: Santa Maria in Cosmedin porch | Style: Ancient Roman sculptural drain cover | Period: AD 14–37

The Mouth of Truth is a famous ancient Roman sculpture depicting the head of a woman with an open mouth, located within the Church of Santa Maria in Cosmedin on the Via del Corso. It has become one of Rome's most iconic symbols. Ancient manhole mask carved as river god face—Hollywood made it an honesty gag. It is believed to be carved from travertine stone. Legend says the sculpture guards against liars by biting off their hand. The site also features the Bocca della Verità Fountain, which contains the sculpture. During the Middle Ages, the area became known for being haunted due to local folklore. Today, visitors can see the sculpture amidst a bustling piazza filled with cafes and shops. Arrive early or wait in sun-exposed line. Circus Maximus and Aventine are walking distance.

Originally created as part of a fountain during the reign of Emperor Tiberius around AD 14–37, the sculpture was rediscovered in 1659 and moved to its current location in 1822. Its name derives from a legend that states anyone who tells lies while placing their hand inside the mouth will have it bitten off. Medieval transfer to the church porch; queue photos keep the myth alive. Visiting the Mouth of Truth offers a glimpse into Rome’s rich historical heritage and provides an opportunity to experience one of the city's lesser-known but intriguing attractions. Quick stop beside the tiny Cosmati-floored church.

Palatine Hill

↑ Contents

Address: Via di San Gregorio / Forum side entrances | Style: Ancient Roman imperial residences | Period: Archaic–Imperial layers

Legendary birthplace of Rome where emperors built palatial complexes above the Forum, with terraces, frescoed rooms, and stadium foundations. House of Augustus and House of Livia preserve delicate wall paintings. Farnese Gardens offer shaded paths and Forum overlooks. Palatine Museum displays sculptures and domestic finds. Wear sturdy shoes—paths are uneven and exposed in summer. Morning visits beat afternoon heat on open slopes.

Huts of Romulus gave way to Republican houses and then imperial domus: Augustus, Tiberius, and Domitian expanded the hill into a government-in-residence overlooking the city. Combined tickets with the Colosseum and Forum unlock the most complete walk through Rome's power centre.

Palazzo Barberini

↑ Contents

Style: Baroque | Period: 1629–1633

It was commissioned by Pope Urban VIII and designed by architect Francesco Borromini between 1629 and 1633. It contains the renowned Galleria Nazionale d'Arte Antica, showcasing masterpieces by artists such as Caravaggio and Raphael. The gardens surrounding the palazzo feature beautiful fountains and statues designed by Bernini himself. Its architecture, blending classical elements with Baroque exuberance, is considered a prime example of Roman Baroque style. In addition to its art collection, the palazzo also features a library with rare manuscripts and historical documents. Today it serves as a museum open to the public, offering visitors a glimpse into the opulent lifestyle of the aristocratic families of the past.

Built during the early 17th century under the patronage of Cardinal Maffeo Barberini, later Pope Urban VIII, the palazzo served as both a private residence and a cultural center for the powerful Barberini family. Its interior boasts lavish decorations, including frescoes by Pietro da Cortona and sculptures by Bernini, reflecting the opulence and artistic prowess of the era. A must-visit for art lovers, the Palazzo Barberini houses one of the most important collections of Baroque art in Rome.

Pantheon

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza della Rotonda | Style: Ancient Roman / early Christian | Period: around 126 AD

The Pantheon is Rome's most iconic temple and one of the best-preserved ancient buildings in the world. Its domed roof, constructed without the use of mortar, stands as a testament to Roman engineering prowess. Domed temple-turned-church with the world's largest unreinforced concrete dome and clear oculus. Its dome is the largest unreinforced concrete dome ever built. It has remained largely intact since its construction nearly two millennia ago. The building was consecrated as a Catholic church in 7th century AD. The oculus is the sole source of daylight within the structure. Restoration work began in the late 17th century under Pope Urban VIII. Mass times suspend tourist circulation briefly. Fountain in the square frames façade photos after rain.

Built around 126 AD by Emperor Hadrian, the Pantheon originally served as a pagan temple dedicated to all gods. It was later converted into a Christian church during the Middle Ages. The oculus at its center allows natural light to flood the interior, creating a breathtaking effect. Hadrianic rebuilding on an earlier plan; Christian consecration saved it from spoliation. Visit the Pantheon for its architectural marvels, historical significance, and stunning interior design. Raphael's tomb and free entry for worship balance with tourist crowding at midday.

Piazza del Campidoglio

↑ Contents

Style: Renaissance | Period: early 16th century

Perched high above Rome's historic center, Piazza del Campidoglio is a majestic square framed by the Palazzo Senatorio and the Capitoline Museums.
