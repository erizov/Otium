Historical overview

↑ Contents

From Rome's ancient forums and Ravenna's mosaics to Milan's and Rome's glass towers of the 21st century, Italian architecture has set European benchmarks at every turn of history.

The Roman Empire left arches, amphitheatres and concrete engineering; early Christian basilicas and Byzantine motifs prepared the Middle Ages. Romanesque abbeys and Italian Gothic with its polychrome marble gave way to the Renaissance of Florence and Rome—from Brunelleschi to Bramante and Michelangelo.

The Baroque of Bernini and Borromini, Sicilian Baroque after 1693 and Napoleonic Neoclassicism shaped cities. Stile Liberty, Rationalism and Fascist-era architecture reflected modernity and politics; post-war modernism, Brutalism and Tendenza postmodernism prepared today's museums and urban interventions.

This guide follows chronological chapters; each lists landmark monuments with brief context and architect names.

1. Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE)

↑ Contents

Temples, forums and arches of ancient Italy; Roman concrete and orders.

Castel Sant'Angelo

↑ Contents

Address: Lungotevere Castello | Style: Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE) | Period: 139 AD

Constructed by Emperor Hadrian around 139 AD to serve as his burial site, the structure evolved into a military stronghold during the Middle Ages. It was subsequently used as a prison and a castle by popes, including Pope Nikolay V who added defensive walls and towers. In 1527, it played a crucial role in defending Rome against attacks by Charles V's troops A must-visit for its historical significance and stunning views overlooking the Tiber River and Vatican City.

Colosseum

↑ Contents

Address: Рим | Style: Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE)

The Flavian Amphitheatre was Rome's largest arena. It seated about fifty thousand spectators; a service labyrinth lies beneath the arena. Symbol of Roman engineering and public spectacle. A landmark of Roman concrete, vaulting and mass spectacle.

Pantheon

↑ Contents

Address: Рим | Style: Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE)

Temple with antiquity's largest unreinforced dome. A concrete dome with the open oculus lights a hall forty-three metres wide. Model of Roman concrete and central planning. The best-preserved great Roman vault; an engineering icon.

Piazza Navona

↑ Contents

Address: Historic centre | Style: Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE) | Period: 16th century

Constructed on the ruins of Emperor Domitian’s stadium, Piazza Navona served as a circus where chariot races were held during ancient Roman times. The name 'Navona' derives from the Latin word 'quadrana nova,' meaning 'new square.' Over time, the piazza became a hub for religious festivals, political gatherings, and social life A must-visit for its stunning Baroque architecture, lively fountains, and charming atmosphere, Piazza Navona offers visitors a glimpse into both ancient and modern Rome.

Roman Forum

↑ Contents

Address: Between Colosseum and Capitoline Hill | Style: Etruscan and Roman architecture (8th c. BCE — 5th c. CE)

Once the political, religious, and commercial center of the Roman Empire, the Roman Forum was the stage for key moments in Roman civilization, including speeches by famous orators like Cicero and Julius Caesar. It housed temples, markets, and government buildings, serving as a hub for civic life until its decline after the fall of the empire Visit the Roman Forum to experience the birthplace of Western democracy and the cradle of Roman law and culture.

2. Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.)

↑ Contents

Ravenna basilicas and mosaics; transition from antiquity to Middle Ages.

Basilica of Santa Croce

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Santa Croce | Style: Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.) | Period: 1294–1442

Built between 1294 and 1442, the basilica was commissioned by the Franciscans as a burial site for prominent Florentine families and notable figures such as Michelangelo, Machiavelli, and Galileo. It became a symbol of religious devotion and civic pride during the Renaissance Visitors come here to admire its architectural grandeur and to pay homage to the illustrious individuals buried within its walls.

Piazza San Marco

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Marco, Venice (San Marco sestiere) | Style: Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.) | Period: 9th century

Originally built in the 9th century, Piazza San Marco was designed to honor St. Mark, the patron saint of Venice. Over time, it became the political and ceremonial center of the Venetian Republic, hosting important gatherings and celebrations. Today, its architecture reflects both Byzantine and Romanesque influences, showcasing the rich historical heritage of Venice Visiting Piazza San Marco allows travelers to immerse themselves in the vibrant atmosphere of Venice while experiencing its unique blend of art, culture, and history.

Basilica of Santa Maria Maggiore

↑ Contents

Address: rome | Style: Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.) | Period: 432–439 AD

Built between 432 and 439 AD by Pope Sixtus III, the Basilica of Santa Maria Maggiore was originally constructed as a celebration of the Virgin Mary's apparition to St. Elena. It has been expanded and restored numerous times over the centuries, including significant renovations under Pope Paul V in the 17th century. The basilica remains a key site in Roman Catholic liturgy and art, housing important religious relics and works of art A must-visit for its historical significance, architectural beauty, and spiritual importance.

Archbasilica of St. John Lateran

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza di San Giovanni in Laterano | Style: Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.) | Period: 4th century; Baroque façade 1732–1735

Emperor Constantine donated the site; the present basilica descends from a 4th-century foundation rebuilt after fires and restorations across the Middle Ages and Renaissance. Borromini reshaped the interior; Alessandro Galilei designed the monumental white façade in the 1730s Mother church of all churches in Rome and the world; pilgrims begin here before other major basilicas.

Santa Maria in Trastevere

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere | Style: Early Christian and Byzantine (4th–8th c.) | Period: 4th century AD

Built around 349 AD by Pope Marcellinus, Santa Maria in Trastevere was originally dedicated to Saint Stephen. Over the centuries, it has undergone several renovations and expansions, particularly during the Renaissance when its stunning mosaics were added. The church also served as a refuge for early Christians during persecutions Visiting Santa Maria in Trastevere offers a glimpse into Roman history and religious heritage, showcasing some of the most exquisite medieval and Renaissance artworks in Rome.

3. Romanesque (11th–12th c.)

↑ Contents

Thick walls, round arches and stone churches of the North and Tuscany.

San Zeno Maggiore

↑ Contents

Address: Верона | Style: Romanesque (11th–12th c.)

Romanesque basilica with triple portal. The triple portal bears twelfth-century reliefs. Verona's foremost Romanesque church. Verona's principal Romanesque church.

San Miniato al Monte

↑ Contents

Address: Флоренция | Style: Romanesque (11th–12th c.)

Romanesque basilica with white and green marble façade. A Christ mosaic and green-white marble façade rise above the Arno. Among Tuscany's best-preserved Romanesque churches. A lofty Romanesque interior with Byzantine wall mosaics.

Sant'Ambrogio

↑ Contents

Address: Милан | Style: Romanesque (11th–12th c.)

Basilica with atrium and brick Romanesque volumes. An atrium of antique columns leads to a hall with mosaics and a timber ceiling. Key monument of Milanese Romanesque. Milan's principal church and a Lombard Romanesque model.

Modena Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Модена | Style: Romanesque (11th–12th c.) | Period: 1099

Romanesque cathedral with Wiligelmo's sculpture. Wiligelmo's portal reliefs narrate biblical scenes in stone. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Lanfranco's cathedral and a Romanesque masterpiece of the Po Valley.

Pisa Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Пиза | Style: Romanesque (11th–12th c.) | Period: 1063

Stone cathedral with the famous leaning bell tower. The marble cathedral was begun in 1063 on the Campo dei Miracoli. Exemplar of Tuscan Romanesque. Core of the ensemble with the Leaning Tower and baptistery.

Zisa

↑ Contents

Summer palace in Arab-Norman style. A summer residence with a fountain hall and Arab-Norman decorative motifs. Islamic planning in palace architecture. Monument of the Sicilian court under William II.

Martorana

↑ Contents

Church with Arab-Norman mosaics. Mosaics blend Byzantine technique with Arab motifs. Jewel of Palermitan architecture.

Palatine Chapel

↑ Contents

Address: Палермо | Style: Norman-Arab architecture of Sicily (12th c.) | Period: 1132

Mosaic chapel of the Norman Palace. Golden mosaics and Arab wooden ceilings adorn the royal chapel. Synthesis of Arab, Byzantine and Latin forms. Jewel of the Norman palace in Palermo.

San Giovanni degli Eremiti

↑ Contents

Church with red domes and cloister garden. A hypogeum with Norman rulers' sarcophagi lies beneath the cathedral floor. Arab domes on a Norman church. Links the Palermo cathedral to royal burials.

Monreale Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Палермо | Style: Norman-Arab architecture of Sicily (12th c.) | Period: 1174

Norman cathedral with golden mosaics. Golden mosaics in the dome and apse cover six thousand square metres. Peak of Norman-Arab synthesis in Sicily. Synthesis of Norman, Byzantine and Arab culture in Sicily.

Palermo Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Палермо | Style: Norman-Arab architecture of Sicily (12th c.) | Period: 1185

Cathedral layering styles from Romanesque to Neoclassical. The façade was rebuilt; inside are mosaics and royal tombs. Sicily's history in stone. Principal church of Norman Palermo.

Cefalù Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Чефалу | Style: Norman-Arab architecture of Sicily (12th c.) | Period: 1131

Founded by Roger II with Byzantine mosaics. A Byzantine Christ Pantocrator in the apse dominates the nave. Early Norman church of Sicily. Norman cathedral on the Tyrrhenian shore.

5. Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.)

↑ Contents

Brick façades, polychrome marble and restrained spires.

Florence Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza del Duomo, Florence | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: 1296–1436

Built between 1296 and 1436, the cathedral was designed by Arnolfo di Cambio and later completed with Filippo Brunelleschi's dome, which remains an engineering marvel. The facade, adorned with marble panels and statues, reflects the city's wealth and artistic prowess during the Renaissance A must-visit for its architectural grandeur and historical significance, Florence Cathedral showcases the pinnacle of medieval craftsmanship and artistry.

Santa Maria Novella

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Santa Maria Novella | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: 1278–1469

Built during the late 14th century, Santa Maria Novella was originally constructed by the Dominican friars. It served as their convent church and became a significant spiritual hub for the community. The interior boasts exquisite frescoes by artists such as Ambrogio Lorenzetti and Andrea Orcagna, showcasing the artistic prowess of the era Visitors come here to admire its stunning architecture and appreciate the rich historical context it represents.

Orsanmichele

↑ Contents

Address: Via Calzaiuoli / Via dell'Arte della Lana | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: 1389–1401

Constructed between 1389 and 1401, Orsanmichele served both practical and religious purposes. It originally functioned as a granary for storing wheat and flour before being transformed into a chapel. The interior boasts stunning sculptures by renowned artists such as Donatello and Andrea Pisano Visitors come to admire its unique design and artistic treasures, including some of the most celebrated works of Florentine sculpture.

Florence Baptistery

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Giovanni, facing the cathedral | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: 1059–1128

Constructed between 1059 and 1128, the Florence Baptistery was originally designed by architect Bindo di Gherardo da Verona. It features three portals decorated with intricate marble panels depicting biblical scenes, including those by Lorenzo Ghiberti and Andrea Pisano. The baptismal font within the structure symbolizes Christian initiation rituals Visiting the Florence Baptistery allows visitors to appreciate its iconic medieval architecture and admire the masterful sculptures that adorn its exterior.

Torre d'Arnolfo

↑ Contents

Address: Palazzo Vecchio tower entrance from courtyard | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: 1298–1334

Built between 1298 and 1334 by Arnolfo di Cambio, Torre d'Arnolfo was originally part of the Palazzo Publico complex. It served as a watchtower and later became a bell tower for the nearby cathedral. The tower's design reflects Gothic influences, showcasing intricate stone carvings and elegant arches Climb to the top of Torre d'Arnolfo for breathtaking vistas of Florence's historic center.

Grand Canal

↑ Contents

Address: S-shaped main waterway through the historic center | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: Natural channel urbanized from the Middle Ages onward

Constructed over centuries, the canal was originally a natural lagoon channel that became part of Venice's urban fabric. It has been widened and deepened to accommodate larger boats and become a focal point of the city's architecture and culture Visiting the Grand Canal allows travelers to experience the heart of Venice, witnessing its unique blend of Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque architecture while enjoying gondola rides and exploring charming bridges and palaces along its shores.

St. Mark's Clock Tower

↑ Contents

Address: North side of Piazza San Marco, Mercerie entrance | Style: Italian Gothic (13th–14th c.) | Period: Late 15th century

Built in the late 15th century during the Renaissance era, the tower originally served as both a clock and a bell tower. It was designed by Antonio Rizzo, a Venetian architect renowned for his innovative use of Gothic elements combined with classical architecture. The tower became famous for its intricate astronomical clock, which displays the movement of celestial bodies, including the sun, moon, and stars Visiting St. Mark’s Clock Tower allows travelers to experience the fusion of art, science, and engineering in one exquisite structure. 6. Early Renaissance (15th c.)

↑ Contents

Florence: proportion, perspective and classical motifs in private palaces.

Uffizi Gallery

↑ Contents

Address: Piazzale degli Uffizi 6 | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: Early 16th century

Built in the early 16th century for Florence's magistrates, the Uffizi was originally intended to be offices ('uffizi' means 'offices'). Over time it became a royal residence and eventually transformed into a museum, housing some of Italy's greatest artistic treasures A must-visit for anyone interested in Renaissance art, the Uffizi offers unparalleled insights into European painting and sculpture.

Palazzo Vecchio

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza della Signoria | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: 1298–1314

Constructed between 1298 and 1314, Palazzo Vecchio originally served as the headquarters for the Florentine Republic. It was later expanded by the Medicis, who transformed it into their ruling palace. The building's facade, designed by Giorgio Vasari, showcases intricate details inspired by classical Roman architecture A must-visit for its historical significance and architectural grandeur, Palazzo Vecchio offers breathtaking views overlooking the city center and houses important artworks by artists such as Michelangelo and Botticelli.

Basilica of San Lorenzo

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Lorenzo | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: 1421–1442

Built between 1421 and 1442 by Filippo Brunelleschi, the basilica was commissioned by Cosimo de' Medici as a burial site for his family. It showcases early Renaissance architecture, blending Gothic elements with classical influences. The interior features intricate marble decorations and tombs designed by Michelangelo, including those of Pope Leo X and Giuliano de' Medici Visiting the Basilica of San Lorenzo allows visitors to explore the fusion of art, architecture, and Florentine history, particularly the contributions of the Medici dynasty.

Palazzo Pitti

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza de' Pitti 1 | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: Early 16th century

Originally constructed for Luca Pitti, a wealthy Florentine banker, the palace was intended to rival the nearby Ducal Palace. However, after being purchased by Cosimo I de' Medici, it underwent extensive renovations under architects such as Vasari and Bernardo Buontalenti. The Medici family used it as their official residence until 1743 when Grand Duke Gian Gastone moved elsewhere Today, Palazzo Pitti houses several museums including the Galleria Palatina, which showcases masterpieces by artists like Raphael, Titian, and Caravaggio, making it essential for art lovers visiting Florence.

Boboli Gardens

↑ Contents

Address: Behind Palazzo Pitti, entrance Via Romana / Pitti | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: late 16th century

Built in the late 16th century by Grand Duke Francesco I de' Medici, these lavish gardens were inspired by Italian Renaissance principles. Designed by renowned architects Niccolò Tribolo and Ammannati, they feature intricate fountains, sculptures, and pathways that reflect the elegance and opulence of the Medicis' era A must-visit for anyone seeking tranquility amidst Florence's bustling streets, Boboli Gardens provide a glimpse into Florentine history while showcasing stunning landscapes and artworks.

Galleria dell'Accademia

↑ Contents

Address: Via Ricasoli 58–60 | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: 1784

Founded in 1784 as part of the Grand Duke Leopoldo's Academy of Fine Arts, the gallery showcases masterpieces collected over centuries. The most famous exhibit is Michelangelo's marble sculpture 'David', which has become one of the city's symbols Visiting Galleria dell'Accademia allows visitors to appreciate the artistic heritage of the Italian Renaissance and admire one of the greatest sculptures ever created.

Palazzo Strozzi

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Strozzi | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: 1489–1504

Commissioned by Francesco di Lorenzo Strozzi around 1489, the palace was designed by architect Michelozzo di Bartolomeo. It served as both a residence for the Strozzi family and later became a venue for cultural gatherings and exhibitions. The building's facade features elegant loggias and elaborate sculptural details, reflecting the artistic sophistication of its era Visiting Palazzo Strozzi offers insight into Florentine history and artistry, showcasing how wealth and power were expressed through architectural grandeur.

Piazza della Signoria

↑ Contents

Address: Between Palazzo Vecchio, Loggia, and Uffizi horn | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: Late Middle Ages

Founded during the late Middle Ages, Piazza della Signoria served as the center of Florentine governance and justice. It was here that significant historical events unfolded, including the famous trial of Savonarola in 1498. The square also houses some of the most celebrated sculptures, notably Michelangelo’s 'David' in the adjacent Loggia del Lanzi Visit Piazza della Signoria to immerse yourself in the city's vibrant history and admire its remarkable artworks.

Villa Farnesina

↑ Contents

Address: rome | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: Early 16th century

Villa Farnesina on the Tiber is an elegant early sixteenth-century villa with outstanding frescoes. Around 1510 Agostino Chigi commissioned Baldassare Peruzzi; Raphael, Sebastiano del Piombo and Sodoma painted mythological cycles in its rooms. The villa preserves High Renaissance unity of architecture and painting. Raphael's Galatea and the Loggia frescoes are landmarks of secular papal Rome.

Quirinal Palace

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza del Quirinale | Style: Early Renaissance (15th c.) | Period: 1583–1585 core; expanded 17th–19th centuries

The Quirinal Palace on its namesake hill is the official residence of the President of Italy. Popes enlarged the palace from 1583; Italian kings lived here after unification and presidents since 1946. State rooms and gardens open for tours on selected days. The changing of the guard draws visitors; the ensemble reflects four centuries of court life.

St Peter's Basilica

↑ Contents

Address: Рим | Style: High Renaissance (early 16th c.) | Period: 1506–1626

St Peter's Basilica in Vatican City is the principal church of Catholicism and one of the largest churches ever built. Work began in 1506 to Donato Bramante's design; Raphael, Antonio da Sangallo the Younger, Michelangelo, Giacomo della Porta and Carlo Maderno later shaped the dome and plan. The façade was finished in 1614 and the interior and forecourt by 1626. Michelangelo's dome defines the Roman skyline; the basilica embodies Counter-Reformation triumph and papal authority.

Teatro Olimpico

Palladio's first Renaissance indoor theatre. The stage with perspectival streets was Andrea Palladio's final project. Influenced European theatre design. Oldest surviving Renaissance indoor theatre.

Uffizi

Vasari's administrative block. Vasari's corridor links the Palazzo Vecchio to the Uffizi. Prototype museum complex. Prototype of the European museum quarter.

Palazzo Thiene

Doge's Palace

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Marco, adjacent to the lagoon | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: 14th–16th century

Built between the 14th and 16th centuries, Doge’s Palace was the seat of the Republic of Venice’s government. It served as both the residence of the Doge, the city's ruler, and the venue for important political gatherings and ceremonies. Notable features include the Hall of the Great Council, where major decisions were made, and the Bridge of Sighs, which connected the palace to the prison, symbolizing the final moment of hope before incarceration A must-visit for its architectural splendor and historical significance, Doge’s Palace offers insights into Venice’s past glory and the intricate workmanship of Renaissance artistry.

Bridge of Sighs

↑ Contents

Address: Linking the Doge's Palace to the former prisons | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: 1602

Constructed in 1602 by architect Baldassarre Longhena, the Bridge of Sighs was originally intended as a route between the interrogation chambers of the prisons and the courtrooms. Its name derives from the belief that prisoners would take their last look at freedom through the windows before being led into their cells below. The bridge’s design reflects Venetian Gothic style with elegant arches and decorative details A must-visit attraction, the Bridge of Sighs offers breathtaking views of St Mark's Square and the Grand Canal, symbolizing the transition from liberty to captivity.

Marciana Library

↑ Contents

Address: Piazzetta San Marco, facing the lagoon | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: late 18th century

Originally founded as a private library for Venetian aristocrats, Marciana Library was later opened to the public during the early 19th century. It became part of the state-owned Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana in 1867, preserving its status as a national institution while maintaining its unique character and charm A must-visit for bibliophiles and historians alike, Marciana Library offers a glimpse into Venice’s intellectual heritage and provides access to invaluable resources.

Jewish Museum of Venice

Address: Campo del Ghetto Nuovo, Cannaregio | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: Museum established 20th c.; synagogues 16th–17th c.

Founded in 2002, the museum showcases artifacts, documents, and artwork that trace the history of Venetian Jewry. It was created to preserve and share the stories of those who lived, worked, and contributed to Venice’s society over centuries Visiting the Jewish Museum allows travelers to gain insight into the unique contributions made by Venice’s Jewish population and their enduring legacy.

Murano

↑ Contents

Address: Lagoon island north of Venice historic center | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: Glassmaking concentrated from 1291 onward

Founded in the 7th century, Murano became renowned for its glass industry during the Middle Ages when Venetian authorities moved glassmakers to the island to protect trade secrets. Over time, Murano developed into a hub of artistic innovation, with master craftsmen creating intricate designs that captivated visitors worldwide Visiting Murano allows travelers to witness firsthand the ancient techniques used by skilled artisans and admire their breathtaking creations.

Venetian Arsenal

↑ Contents

Address: Castello sestiere, eastern edge of the historic city | Style: Palladian and Venetian Renaissance (16th c.) | Period: late 13th century

Founded in the late 13th century, the Venetian Arsenal became one of Europe’s earliest industrial complexes. It played a crucial military and economic role for centuries, producing ships for both trade and defense. At its peak, it employed thousands of workers and could produce up to ten warships simultaneously. Its layout included specialized workshops, storage areas, and docking facilities, showcasing advanced engineering techniques and organization Today, the Arsenal is a testament to Venice's maritime heritage and technological prowess during the Renaissance.

San Giorgio Maggiore

Constructed between 1609 and 1617, San Giorgio Maggiore was commissioned by Cardinal Giovanni Maffeo Barbarigo to serve as a religious retreat. The church's design reflects Palladio's classical principles, blending Roman elements with Venetian Gothic architecture. Its iconic bell tower, crowned by a statue of St. George, became a symbol of Venice’s architectural prowess Visitors come here to admire the harmonious blend of architecture and nature, offering panoramic vistas of the Grand Canal and the lagoon.

Giardini della Biennale

↑ Contents

Address: Castello edge, Biennale gardens | Style: Contemporary architecture (2000s–present) | Period: 1909

Originally built in 1909 to showcase modern decorative arts during the first Venice Biennale, the Giardini della Biennale has evolved into a world-renowned venue for international art exhibitions. Over time, it has hosted numerous groundbreaking shows that have shaped the global art scene Visiting the Giardini della Biennale allows visitors to experience cutting-edge art exhibits and gain insight into current artistic trends.

Punta della Dogana

↑ Contents

Address: Triangular point between Grand Canal and Giudecca Canal | Style: Contemporary architecture (2000s–present) | Period: 1549

Constructed in the early 16th century, Punta della Dogana served as a customs station for goods entering and leaving Venice. Its design reflects the architectural elegance typical of the Renaissance era, with intricate stone carvings and elegant arches. The building was later converted into a museum by the Fondazione Peggy Guggenheim in 2009, showcasing modern and contemporary art collections A must-visit for its breathtaking views over the Grand Canal and the opportunity to explore avant-garde art exhibitions.

MAXXI

↑ Contents

Address: Рим | Style: Contemporary architecture (2000s–present) | Period: 2009

MAXXI is Rome's National Museum of 21st Century Arts, designed by Zaha Hadid. It opened in 2010 on a former Montello barracks site. Flowing concrete bands, bridges and ramps create continuous gallery space for exhibitions and events. Italy's first museum devoted exclusively to contemporary art and architecture.
