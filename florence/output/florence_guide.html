OTIUM

OTIUM is the practice of deliberate leisure. In the classical sense, otium is not escape from work but time when attention returns to memory, landscape, and thought — without a utilitarian deadline.

We shape routes for lingering, not for checklist tourism. The goal is presence in a place rather than consumption of sights.

OTIUM is not a tour operator. It is an invitation to walk, pause, and leave a route unfinished if the light on a façade deserves another minute.

Basilica della Santissima Annunziata

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza della Santissima Annunziata | Style: Renaissance piazza ensemble; Michelozzo court | Period: 13th c. Servite; 15th c. squares and portico

The Basilica della Santissima Annunziata is a majestic church in Florence, known for its soaring dome and intricate interior decorations. Miracle-working Annunciation image draws ex-votos; Innocenti façade across square completes the humanist frame. It houses the Chapel of Santa Croce, containing the tombs of notable figures such as Vittorio Emanuele II and Queen Margherita. The exterior boasts a striking white-and-green marble façade, inspired by ancient Roman architecture. Inside, visitors can admire the grand dome, which rises high above the nave, providing a sense of awe and reverence. The basilica's museum displays sculptures and artworks from various periods, including works by Donatello and Bernini. Each year, it hosts important religious ceremonies and cultural events. Giambologna bronze center of equestrian Ferdinando I faces church. Hospital degli Innocenti by Brunelleschi balances the north side.

Built between 1250 and 1469, the basilica was originally designed by Arnolfo di Cambio and later completed by Michelozzo di Bartolomeo. It served as a symbol of religious devotion and a burial site for notable Florentine families. The interior features stunning frescoes by artists like Domenico Ghirlandaio and Vasari, depicting scenes from the life of the Virgin Mary. Servite miracle cult met Medici dynastic birthday celebrations in uniform architecture. This basilica is significant for its architectural beauty and historical importance, showcasing the evolution of Gothic and Renaissance styles. First synchronous Renaissance square composition in Florence.

Basilica of San Lorenzo

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Lorenzo | Style: Early Renaissance basilica (Brunelleschi plan) | Period: Church c. 1419–1470s core; unfinished façade

The Basilica of San Lorenzo is one of Florence's most significant religious landmarks, renowned for its association with the Medici family and their tombs within. Medici chapels include Michelangelo New Sacristy sculptures; the Laurentian Library entrance stair shocks nearby. It is located near the Piazza San Lorenzo, adjacent to the Uffizi Gallery. The basilica houses the oldest known baptismal font in Florence. Michelangelo’s tomb designs are considered masterpieces of Renaissance sculpture. Its facade features sculptures by Andrea Verrocchio. The building has undergone several restorations over the centuries. Parish church of the Medici before ducal power. Rough stone west front was never dressed in marble as planned.

Built between 1421 and 1442 by Filippo Brunelleschi, the basilica was commissioned by Cosimo de' Medici as a burial site for his family. It showcases early Renaissance architecture, blending Gothic elements with classical influences. The interior features intricate marble decorations and tombs designed by Michelangelo, including those of Pope Leo X and Giuliano de' Medici. Cosimo the Elder financed Brunelleschi interventions; popes and bankers enlarged burial display. Visiting the Basilica of San Lorenzo allows visitors to explore the fusion of art, architecture, and Florentine history, particularly the contributions of the Medici dynasty. Architectural index of Medici taste from sober brick to mannerist marble.

Basilica of Santa Croce

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Santa Croce | Style: Italian Gothic hall church; Bardi and Peruzzi chapels | Period: Begun 1294; neo-Gothic façade 1863

One of Florence's most iconic landmarks, the Basilica of Santa Croce is a testament to Gothic architecture and one of Italy’s largest churches. Its soaring nave and vaulted ceilings are adorned with frescoes by some of Italy’s greatest artists. Pantheon of Italian genius: tombs of Michelangelo, Galileo, Machiavelli, and Rossini line the nave. It houses the tombs of famous historical figures including Michelangelo, Dante Alighieri, and Niccolò Machiavelli. The basilica features impressive fresco cycles by Giotto and other renowned artists. Its exterior is characterized by intricate decorative elements and four massive bell towers. Santa Croce underwent extensive restoration work in the late 20th century to preserve its artistic heritage. The interior boasts a striking layout with three aisles and a central dome. Franciscan rival to Dominican Santa Maria Novella across town. Flood of 1966 heavily damaged Cimabue crucifix; conservation became a model.

Built between 1294 and 1442, the basilica was commissioned by the Franciscans as a burial site for prominent Florentine families and notable figures such as Michelangelo, Machiavelli, and Galileo. It became a symbol of religious devotion and civic pride during the Renaissance. Patrician families competed in side chapels with fresco cycles by Giotto school artists. Visitors come here to admire its architectural grandeur and to pay homage to the illustrious individuals buried within its walls. Moral center for civic humanism and memorial sculpture.

Basilica of Santa Maria del Carmine

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza del Carmine, Oltrarno | Style: Plain Carmelite exterior; baroque renovation parts | Period: 13th c. core; Brancacci Chapel frescoes 1424–1428

The Basilica of Santa Maria del Carmine is a solemn and imposing church located in the heart of Florence's historic center. It stands as one of the city's most notable examples of Gothic architecture, characterized by its intricate vaulting and elegant facade. Masaccio and Masolino Expulsion and Tribute Money revolutionized narrative depth; ticketed timed visits. Completed around 1389, the basilica is considered a significant example of late medieval Italian Gothic architecture. It houses the tomb of St. Masaccio's frescos depict scenes from the life of Christ and are considered groundbreaking for their realistic portrayal of figures. The interior features stunning decorative elements such as marble columns and elaborate stucco work. The basilica also contains a chapel dedicated to St. Fire 1771 destroyed much nave; chapel survived as study piece. Filippino Lippi completed chapel after Masaccio's death.

Built during the late 14th century, the basilica was originally constructed to house the remains of St. Catherine of Siena. Over time, it became known for its exquisite frescoes by Masaccio, including his famous 'Trinity' painting, which revolutionized early Renaissance art with its innovative use of perspective and realism. Merchant Brancacci commissioned humanist salvation stories for a family chapel. Visitors come here to admire the masterpiece of Masaccio's fresco cycle and experience the serene atmosphere of one of Florence’s oldest churches. Art-history classrooms cite these walls for linear perspective birth.

Basilica of Santo Spirito

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Santo Spirito, Oltrarno | Style: Brunelleschi Latin-cross plan, whitewashed interior | Period: 1444–1487

The Basilica of Santo Spirito is a prominent example of Renaissance architecture in Florence, blending classical elements with Gothic influences. Pure proportioned geometry; Saturday market and aperitivo life fill the sleepy piazza outside. Commissioned by the wool workers' guild (Arte della Lana). Features a distinctive polychrome marble facade. Contains frescoes by artists such as Domenico Ghirlandaio. Dome designed by Giuliano da Maiano. Renovated several times over centuries but retains its original structure. Michelangelo dissected corpses in convent cells borrowed nearby. Crucifix attributed to youthful Michelangelo.

Built between 1439 and 1481, the basilica was commissioned by the Florentine guilds to commemorate their patron saint, Saint Spirit. Designed by architect Michelozzo di Bartolomeo, it features a striking façade inspired by ancient Roman temples and a grand interior with a nave and two aisles supported by elegant columns. The basilica's dome, completed by Giuliano da Maiano, stands as one of the earliest examples of dome construction in Italy. Augustinian house rebuilt after fire with mathematic calm in plan and pietra serena lines. Visiting the Basilica of Santo Spirito allows visitors to appreciate the fusion of architectural styles that define the transition from medieval to Renaissance design. Brunelleschi's spiritual architecture at human scale in the artisans' quarter.

Boboli Gardens

↑ Contents

Location: Behind Palazzo Pitti, entrance Via Romana / Pitti | Style: Italian Mannerist and Baroque formal garden | Period: 16th–18th c. terracing and bosquets

Perched above the historical center of Florence, Boboli Gardens offer a serene escape into nature. These elegant gardens were originally designed as a private retreat for the Medici family during the 16th century and later became a public park open to all citizens. Caves, grottoes, twin-ram fountains, and long cypress alleys model princely absolutism on a slope. The gardens span approximately 48 acres (19 hectares). They are home to over 7,000 plants and trees. Notable features include the Fountain of Neptune and the Gallery of Statues. Accessible via Via Romana or Piazza Antinori. Open daily from dawn until dusk. Buontalenti Grotto and Ammannati Neptune basin are highlights. Linked to Forte Belvedere for military sightlines.

Built in the late 16th century by Grand Duke Francesco I de' Medici, these lavish gardens were inspired by Italian Renaissance principles. Designed by renowned architects Niccolò Tribolo and Ammannati, they feature intricate fountains, sculptures, and pathways that reflect the elegance and opulence of the Medicis' era. Medici wives and gardeners imported citrus and hydraulics from villa tradition. A must-visit for anyone seeking tranquility amidst Florence's bustling streets, Boboli Gardens provide a glimpse into Florentine history while showcasing stunning landscapes and artworks. Open-air sculpture gallery influencing European court gardens.

Casa Buonarroti

↑ Contents

Address: Via Ghibellina 70 | Style: Baroque palace front over medieval plots | Period: 1612 house-museum on Michelangelo heirs' property

Casa Buonarroti is a museum dedicated to the life and works of Michelangelo, located in Florence's historic center. It showcases his early sculptures and personal belongings, offering visitors a unique glimpse into the artist’s formative years. Models, drawings, and juvenilia include Battle of the Centaurs relief; research library upstairs. The museum displays some of Michelangelo's earliest known sculptures, including 'Madonna of the Steps'. It also contains sketches and preparatory drawings for famous works like David and Moses. Visitors can see the artist's bedroom and study where he lived and worked. Casa Buonarroti features a small garden with statues inspired by ancient Roman art. The building itself is an example of Renaissance architecture. Michelangelo bought adjacent lots but did not build this exact façade. Nephews turned family prestige into public memorial institution.

Built by Giorgio Vasari in 1566 as a residence for Alessandro di Lorenzo de' Medici, Casa Buonarroti later became home to Michelangelo during his final years. The building was renamed after the Buonarotti family, who purchased it in the late 19th century. Today, it houses a collection of Michelangelo's early marble sculptures, drawings, and personal effects. Legal battles over Michelangelo estate echo in what survived the workshop fire risk. This museum provides insight into Michelangelo's early career and creative development before he achieved worldwide fame. Most intimate Florentine stop for Michelangelo paper and marble beginnings.

Florence Baptistery

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza San Giovanni, facing the cathedral | Style: Romanesque octagon with marble revetment | Period: 11th–12th c. structure; Gates of Paradise 1425–1452

The Florence Baptistery, also known as the Baptisterio di San Giovanni, is a prominent landmark and one of the oldest buildings in Florence, Italy. Its stunning design and rich historical significance make it a must-visit for art lovers and architecture enthusiasts. Florentines still argue if Dante's noble ancient temple stood here; mosaics crown the octagon interior. It is located adjacent to the Duomo Cathedral in the historic center of Florence. Its dome is one of the earliest examples of Gothic architecture in Italy. Each of its three doors has been adorned with impressive bronze reliefs. The interior houses the famous Fontana di San Giovanni, used for baptisms since the 14th century. In 1982, UNESCO designated the entire cathedral complex, including the Baptistery, as a World Heritage Site. Lorenzo Ghiberti east doors (replicas outside, originals in museum). Mosaic cupola includes Last Judgment tiers ascending overhead.

Constructed between 1059 and 1128, the Florence Baptistery was originally designed by architect Bindo di Gherardo da Verona. It features three portals decorated with intricate marble panels depicting biblical scenes, including those by Lorenzo Ghiberti and Andrea Pisano. The baptismal font within the structure symbolizes Christian initiation rituals. Every citizen-notable baptism occurred here until the cathedral font moved indoors later. Visiting the Florence Baptistery allows visitors to appreciate its iconic medieval architecture and admire the masterful sculptures that adorn its exterior. Biblical storytelling in bronze relief defined public competition for artists.

Florence Cathedral

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza del Duomo, Florence | Style: Gothic nave with Brunelleschi double-shell dome | Period: Cathedral 1296–1436; dome 1420–1436; façade 19th c.

Dominating the skyline of Florence, Florence Cathedral, also known as Santa Maria del Fiore, is a majestic symbol of Italian Gothic architecture and one of the largest cathedrals in Europe. Santa Maria del Fiore anchors the religious heart of Florence; climb the dome or Giotto campanile for tight views over tiles and terracotta. The dome, constructed without scaffolding, stands at 114 meters high and is considered one of the greatest achievements of early Renaissance engineering. The facade features intricate marble decorations and sculptures created by artists such as Andrea Pisano and Luca della Robbia. Inside, the cathedral houses Giotto’s famous frescoes on the Apse and Duccio’s altarpiece. The bell tower, known as Campanile di Giotto, is another iconic Florentine landmark designed by Giotto himself. The exterior walls are clad in white, green, and rose-colored marbles, creating a harmonious color scheme. Brunelleschi engineered the dome without centering using herringbone brick. The exterior gained its polychrome marble cladding over centuries.

Built between 1296 and 1436, the cathedral was designed by Arnolfo di Cambio and later completed with Filippo Brunelleschi's dome, which remains an engineering marvel. The facade, adorned with marble panels and statues, reflects the city's wealth and artistic prowess during the Renaissance. Competition and faction among guilds shaped the vast ship-hull nave; Medici patronage later intertwined with republican ritual. A must-visit for its architectural grandeur and historical significance, Florence Cathedral showcases the pinnacle of medieval craftsmanship and artistry. Structural emblem of the Renaissance and the Tuscan skyline.

Fountain of Neptune

↑ Contents

Location: Piazza della Signoria, northwest basin | Style: Mannerist marble fountain with bronze figures | Period: 1559–1575 Ammannati and collaborators

The Fountain of Neptune is a majestic Baroque masterpiece located in the Piazza della Signoria, Florence's historical heart. It was commissioned by Grand Duke Ferdinando I de' Medici and designed by Giambologna in 1567. Sea god and satyrs wrestle sea monsters at the civic heart; restaurateurs ring the piazza. It is one of the most iconic landmarks in Florence’s historic center. Neptune's chariot is supported by six dolphins, representing harmony and balance. The fountain's design reflects the opulence and elegance typical of Baroque art. Each Triton holds a trumpet made from seashells, showcasing intricate detailing. The sculpture exemplifies the fusion of classical mythology with contemporary architectural style. Mocked as white giant on unfinished block in its day. One of several copies of Perseus head spout stories in Cellini memoirs.

Completed in 1567, the fountain celebrates the power and dominion of Neptune, god of the sea. The central figure, Neptune himself, stands atop a shell-shaped chariot pulled by two sea horses, symbolizing his control over the oceans. Surrounding him are four Tritons blowing trumpets, adding to the drama and grandeur of the sculpture. Cosimo I maritime propaganda and marriage alliance symbolism wet the stone. Visiting the Fountain of Neptune allows visitors to appreciate the exquisite craftsmanship and artistic vision of the Renaissance era. Humid counterweight to dry equestrian absolutism meters away.

Galleria dell'Accademia

↑ Contents

Address: Via Ricasoli 58–60 | Style: Former convent and hospital rooms adapted as gallery | Period: 1784 academy; Tribune for David 1873

The Galleria dell'Accademia is a world-famous art museum located in Florence, Italy. It houses some of the most iconic works by Michelangelo and other Renaissance artists. Michelangelo's David dominates; unfinished slaves flank; musical instrument collection is a quiet bonus. It was originally built as an academy for training artists and preserving historical artifacts. In addition to David, it contains paintings by Botticelli, Rosselli, and others. The building itself features neoclassical architecture designed by Giuseppe Poggi. Regularly hosts temporary exhibitions showcasing lesser-known works from its extensive collection. Admission includes access to both permanent collections and special exhibits. David moved from Piazza Signoria to protect marble from weather. Long lines—reserve entry to avoid heat and crowds.

Founded in 1784 as part of the Grand Duke Leopoldo's Academy of Fine Arts, the gallery showcases masterpieces collected over centuries. The most famous exhibit is Michelangelo's marble sculpture 'David', which has become one of the city's symbols. Grand Duke Pietro Leopoldo founded an art school collection; 19th c. nation-building made David civic icon. Visiting Galleria dell'Accademia allows visitors to appreciate the artistic heritage of the Italian Renaissance and admire one of the greatest sculptures ever created. Single best encounter with Michelangelo giant scale in situ.

Loggia dei Lanzi

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza della Signoria, south edge | Style: Late Gothic open loggia | Period: 1376–1382

The Loggia dei Lanzi is a Renaissance-era open-air art gallery in Florence, Italy. It was constructed during the late 14th century and named after the Lanzi family who commissioned its construction. Cellini Perseus and Giambologna Rape of the Sabines crowd marble under triple arches—open-air free museum. Constructed in the late Gothic style with Tuscan elements typical of Florentine architecture. Located on Piazza della Signoria adjacent to Palazzo Vecchio, one of Florence’s most iconic landmarks. Hosted various historical events, including public executions and political gatherings. Renowned for its grand porticoes and stone arches supporting marble slabs. Home to several notable sculptural masterpieces. Begun after union riots as ceremonial guard space. Medici dukes used it for investitures and public display.

Built between 1376 and 1382 by artist Michelozzo di Bartolomeo for the Florentine Republic, the loggia originally served as a meeting space for the city's magistrates. Over time, it became a prominent location for displaying sculptures, including celebrated works such as Giambologna's 'Perseus with the Head of Medusa' and Mudie-Cristie's 'Judith Beheading Holofernes'. Republican meeting point absorbed triumphal statuary programs of principate. A must-visit for art lovers, the Loggia offers a breathtaking collection of classical statues and a unique outdoor setting that enhances their beauty. Sculpture syntax for the entire square's political allegory.

Mercato Centrale

↑ Contents

Address: Via dell'Ariento, San Lorenzo (indoor hall) | Style: 19th-c. iron architecture with basilica plan | Period: 1874 iron-and-glass market hall (Castellazzi)

Located at the heart of Florence's historic center, Mercato Centrale is a vibrant market hall that combines traditional produce stalls with modern design elements. Built in the early 20th century, it serves as both a bustling marketplace and a cultural landmark. Ground floor produce stalls; upstairs food court and cooking classes; wraps traditional San Lorenzo street leather market. Opened in 1914 after years of planning and construction. Combines traditional Italian market stalls with contemporary design aesthetics. Home to various food vendors selling fresh fruits, vegetables, meats, cheeses, and pastries. Features a café area where visitors can enjoy coffee and light meals. Regularly hosts special events such as cooking demonstrations and festivals. Part of Florence capital renewal and rail-era hygiene reform. Recent redevelopment added evening gastonomy without losing vendors.

Established in 1914, Mercato Centrale was designed by architect Giuseppe Martelli to replace the old central market that had been destroyed during World War I. The building features Art Nouveau influences and neoclassical elements, showcasing elegant arches and intricate decorations. Grand-ducal open markets moved under cover as population density rose. Visiting Mercato Centrale offers a unique opportunity to experience authentic Florentine cuisine and local crafts while immersed in the city's rich architectural heritage. Working culinary heart behind the tourist leather arcades.

Museo Galileo

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza dei Giudici 1 | Style: Palazzo rooms as science museum display | Period: Institute 1927; palace of Castellani medieval

The Museo Galileo is a world-class museum dedicated to the history of science and technology, showcasing instruments, models, and artifacts that trace humanity's journey toward understanding the natural world. Telescopes, globes, and Medici scientific instruments next to the Arno; interactive demos for families. It features more than 3,500 objects related to astronomy, physics, mathematics, and medicine. The collection includes some of the earliest telescopes used for astronomical observations. Exhibits highlight the contributions of pioneers like Galileo himself, who revolutionized our understanding of motion and celestial bodies. Visitors can explore interactive exhibits demonstrating principles of mechanics and optics. The museum also hosts temporary exhibitions on various topics within the realm of science. Holds Galileo's finger relic alongside real calculation tools. Merger of Istituto e Museo di Storia della Scienza rebranded Museo Galileo.

Established in 1927 as the Museum of Instruments and Experiments, it was later renamed after its most famous benefactor, Galileo Galilei. The museum moved into its current location in 2001, occupying part of the Palazzo Castellani, a historic building dating back to the early Renaissance era. It houses one of the largest collections of scientific instruments in Europe, including works by notable scientists such as Copernicus, Kepler, and Newton. Tuscan court science collided with ecclesiastical trial memory in collecting choices. Visiting the Museo Galileo offers insight into the evolution of scientific thought and technological innovation over centuries. Premier Italian museum for early modern instruments and optics.

Museo Nazionale del Bargello

↑ Contents

Address: Via del Proconsolo 4 | Style: Medieval civic barracks with cortile | Period: 1255 fortress; museum from 1865

The Museo Nazionale del Bargello is a historic art museum located in Florence's Piazza del Bargello, showcasing Renaissance masterpieces and sculptures by Michelangelo, Donatello, and others. Donatello bronzes, Michelangelo Bacchus, and maiolica rooms pack the old police headquarters. It was originally constructed as a palace for Florentine magistrates in 1255. Today it houses over 400 sculptures and paintings dating back to the 13th through 18th centuries. Notable exhibits include Michelangelo’s 'David' and Donatello’s 'Judith and Holofernes'. The museum underwent extensive renovations in the mid-20th century to preserve its historical integrity and enhance visitor accessibility. Located within walking distance of major landmarks such as the Duomo and the Uffizi Gallery. Captain of the People and later Bargello magistrate held court here. Sculpture competition pieces with the cathedral buttresses stored conceptually here.

Originally built as the Palazzo del Popolo in the late 13th century, the building later became known as the Bargello Palace during the Medici era. It served variously as a prison, a courthouse, and eventually a museum dedicated to Italian art. Today it houses one of Italy’s most important collections of sculpture, including works by some of the greatest artists of the Renaissance. Duke suppression of republican militia repurposed jails into Italy's first national sculpture museum. This museum is significant for its collection of early Renaissance sculptures, particularly those by Michelangelo and Donatello. Donatello scholarship begins in these vaulted rooms.

Ognissanti

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza Ognissanti, Lungarno | Style: Baroque front on Gothic hall | Period: 1251 founding; baroque façade 17th c.

Ognissanti is a solemn and serene church nestled within the historic heart of Florence, known for its rich architectural heritage and intricate interior design. Vespucci and Botticelli tombs; Ghirlandaio Last Supper refectory (separate booking) nearby in complex. The church’s facade is adorned with statues by Donatello depicting saints and biblical figures. Inside, the frescoes by Masaccio portray scenes from the life of Christ, showcasing innovative use of perspective and light. Ognissanti served as a prototype for many subsequent churches in Italy due to its advanced structural techniques. It is also notable for being one of the first buildings in Florence to feature a polygonal plan instead of the traditional rectangular layout. In the 18th century, the church underwent significant renovations under Giuseppe Poggi. Umiliati order church before Vallombrosan observance. Americas name echo in Amerigo Vespucci chapel memorial.

Built during the late Gothic era around 1439, Ognissanti was originally designed by Filippo Brunelleschi, who later became famous for his work on the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore. The church's facade features exquisite sculptures by Donatello, while its interior boasts a remarkable fresco cycle by Masaccio, one of the earliest examples of Renaissance art. Wool guild and admirals' families patronized this Lungarno spiritual anchor. Visiting Ognissanti allows travelers to immerse themselves in the vibrant artistic legacy of early Florentine architecture and painting. Harbor edge spirituality linking mercantile risk to mendicant vows.

Orsanmichele

↑ Contents

Address: Via Calzaiuoli / Via dell'Arte della Lana | Style: Gothic box on pillars with sculpture niches | Period: Grain loggia converted 1337–1404; Andrea Orcagna tabernacle

Situated at the heart of Florence's medieval district, Orsanmichele is a remarkable example of late Gothic architecture. Originally built as a grain storehouse, it was later converted into a church dedicated to Saint Mikhail the Archangel. Once open market hall; guild statues by Donatello, Ghiberti, and Verrocchio ring exterior niches. The exterior features twelve statues representing various trades and professions. Inside, there are four large altarpieces depicting scenes from the life of Saint Mikhail. Donatello's bronze statue of Saint George is one of the highlights within the church. Andrea Pisano's pulpit, adorned with intricate reliefs, stands prominently near the entrance. It was consecrated on May 6, 1401. Madonna delle Grazie altarpiece still venerated inside. Each trade guild sponsored a patron saint figure.

Constructed between 1389 and 1401, Orsanmichele served both practical and religious purposes. It originally functioned as a granary for storing wheat and flour before being transformed into a chapel. The interior boasts stunning sculptures by renowned artists such as Donatello and Andrea Pisano. Miracle image and civic famine control merged commerce and cult in the street grid. Visitors come to admire its unique design and artistic treasures, including some of the most celebrated works of Florentine sculpture. Outdoor sculpture museum of quattrocento Florence between banks.

Palazzo Medici Riccardi

↑ Contents

Address: Via Cavour 3 | Style: Early Renaissance rusticated palace | Period: 1444–1484 Michelozzo; Baroque Riccardi wing 1679

The Palazzo Medici Riccardi is a Renaissance masterpiece located on Via Larga in Florence's historic center. It was commissioned by Lorenzo de' Medici, known as 'Il Magnifico,' and designed by architect Michelozzo di Bartolomeo around 1444. Cappella dei Magi frescoes by Benozzo Gozzoli process along the intimate chapel; courtyard evokes Roman oeci. Commissioned by Lorenzo de' Medici in 1444. Designed by Michelozzo di Bartolomeo. Former home of the Medici family. Currently houses the Museo di Palazzo Riccardi. Located near the Duomo and Piazza della Signoria. Cosimo the Elder chose restrained scale before Pitti ambitions. Later Riccardi family added stucco Galleria and mirror hall.

Built during the early 15th century, the palace served as the residence of the powerful Medici family until it became the seat of the Republic of Florence under Giuliano de' Medici. Its intricate stone façade, richly decorated with sculptures and reliefs, reflects the artistic flourishing of the Florentine Renaissance. Prototype for Florentine palace typology: corner rusticate, cortile, grand salone stack. Visitors should explore the Palazzo Medici Riccardi to appreciate its architectural significance and glimpse into the opulent lifestyle of the Medicis. Medici power before they became dukes, readable in stone.

Palazzo Pitti

↑ Contents

Address: Piazza de' Pitti 1 | Style: Rusticated Renaissance block; later Baroque wings | Period: 1458 banker palace; expanded under Medici and Lorraine

Situated on the banks of the Arno River, Palazzo Pitti is a grand Renaissance palace that stands as one of Florence's most iconic landmarks.
