Historical overview

Saint Petersburg is Russia's "northern capital" — a city of grandeur and culture founded as a bold statement of imperial ambition. Known as the "Venice of the North" for its network of canals and waterways, it is a must-visit destination that combines European elegance with Russian soul.

History of foundation and development. The city was founded by Tsar Peter the Great on 27 May 1703 with the construction of the Peter and Paul Fortress on Zayachy (Hare's) Island in the Neva River delta. The name honours the apostle Peter, the tsar's patron saint.

In 1712 Saint Petersburg becomes the capital of the Russian Empire, shifting political power from Moscow. Peter the Great envisioned it as a "window to Europe" — a modern, Western-facing city and base for Russia's Baltic Fleet. Through the 18th century the city grows rapidly under successive rulers: grand palaces, wide avenues and canals are built, and European architects, artists and craftsmen are drawn to the new capital.

In the 19th century Saint Petersburg solidifies its status as a cultural and intellectual hub. Landmarks like St Isaac's Cathedral and the Alexander Column are erected; the city becomes a centre of literature, art and revolutionary ideas. In 1914 it is renamed Petrograd amid World War I sentiment against German-sounding names.

In 1917 the October Revolution unfolds in the city; the Aurora cruiser's shot signals the storming of the Winter Palace. In 1924 the city is renamed Leningrad after Vladimir Lenin's death. During 1941–1944 the Siege of Leningrad lasts 872 days; over a million civilians perish, but the city does not surrender — an episode central to its modern identity. In 1991 the historic name Saint Petersburg is restored following a public referendum.

Tourist appeal. Saint Petersburg is one of Russia's top tourist destinations, attracting millions annually. The Historic Centre of Saint Petersburg and Related Groups of Monuments holds UNESCO World Heritage status for its outstanding universal value.

Grand palaces, cathedrals and monuments reflect three centuries of tsarist rule. The Hermitage alone holds over three million items, rivalling the great museums of Europe. From late May to mid-July the White Nights bring near twenty-four-hour daylight and a dreamlike atmosphere. Boat tours and the spectacle of bridge lifting at night are quintessential experiences. The Mariinsky Theatre, the White Nights Festival and the "Scarlet Sails" graduation celebration draw visitors throughout the year.

Must-visit attractions. The Peter and Paul Fortress is the city's birthplace; its cathedral houses the tombs of Russian emperors, and the daily noon cannon shot is a long-standing tradition.

The Hermitage is one of the world's largest and oldest museums, housed in the opulent Winter Palace, with collections from ancient art to modern masters. Palace Square and the Arch of the General Staff form the grand ensemble around the Hermitage, dominated by the Alexander Column.

St Isaac's Cathedral is a colossal 19th-century church whose colonnade offers panoramic views. The Church of the Savior on Spilled Blood, built on the site where Tsar Alexander II was assassinated, is famous for colourful onion domes and intricate mosaics. Nevsky Prospect is the city's main artery, lined with shops, cafes, churches and historic buildings.

Kronstadt is a fortified island city and former naval base, accessible by ferry, with the grand Kronstadt Naval Cathedral. Day trips to Peterhof (the "Russian Versailles" with its fountains), Tsarskoe Selo (home to the Amber Room) and Pavlovsk offer glimpses of imperial life beyond the centre.

Current tourism trends. As a major Baltic Sea port, Saint Petersburg welcomes international cruise ships, especially in summer. Literary tours (Dostoevsky, Pushkin), music festivals and ballet performances attract dedicated audiences. Ongoing conservation of palaces and monuments preserves heritage for future visitors. Virtual tours, mobile apps and augmented reality complement traditional sightseeing. White Nights (May–July) and New Year celebrations are peak periods; winter offers ice skating and festive markets.

Tips for tourists. May to September brings mild weather and long days; late May to mid-July is ideal for the White Nights; December and January suit festive decorations and winter activities.

The metro is deep and efficient; buses and river trams in summer offer scenic routes; taxi apps are convenient for longer trips. Pre-book Hermitage and suburban palace tickets online to skip queues; a museum pass can cover several attractions. Hotels near Nevsky Prospect or Palace Square keep major sights within walking distance; central metro access simplifies travel. Offline maps, a phrasebook app and tourist information centres help with orientation upon arrival.

Saint Petersburg offers a journey through Russian history and culture, where every street corner whispers stories of tsars, revolutionaries and artists. From imperial splendour to the resilience of the Siege, its legacy continues to inspire travellers worldwide.
